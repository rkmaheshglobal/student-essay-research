## Step 2: Research Phase (Weeks 1–2)

### Free Academic Sources
- **Google Scholar** (scholar.google.com) — search for key thinkers
- **JSTOR** (jstor.org) — free access to many articles
- **Project MUSE** — humanities journals
- **PhilPapers** — philosophy papers
- **SSRN** — social science working papers
- **Your school library** — ask librarian for database access

### Reading Strategy
1. Start with the **professor-recommended books** for your chosen prompt (listed on CCIR website)
2. Read introductions and conclusions first — get the argument
3. Find **2–3 key statistics or data points** that support your thesis
4. Find **1–2 strong counterarguments** you must address
5. Identify **1 original angle** that most essays won't take

### Note-Taking Template
For each source:
```
Author, Title, Year
Core argument:
Key quote (with page number):
How it supports/challenges my thesis:
```

## Step 3: Thesis Development

### What Makes a Winning Thesis?
- **Specific** — not "populism is complex" but "populism is only destructive when it lacks institutional constraints"
- **Arguable** — judges must be able to disagree with it
- **Supported** — you have evidence for it
- **Original** — it's not the obvious answer

### Thesis Formula
> "Although [common view / counterargument], [your position] because [reason 1], [reason 2], and [reason 3]."

### Example Theses by Prompt

**Prompt 3 (AI legal personhood):**
> "Although AI systems increasingly exhibit human-like reasoning, granting them legal personhood would create accountability gaps that undermine rather than strengthen human oversight — a more effective approach is enhanced corporate liability for AI developers."

**Prompt 4 (Colonial artifacts):**
> "While universal museum arguments have merit, the case for repatriation is stronger: colonial acquisition was rarely consensual, and modern preservation technology eliminates the practical objections to return."

**Prompt 6 (Open borders):**
> "Open borders are morally justified as a matter of basic human freedom, but their implementation requires phased institutional reform — the moral case is clear even if the political path is not."

**Prompt 9 (Healing):**
> "Healing is not the restoration of a prior state but the creation of a new relationship with loss — a process that is irreducibly social, not merely medical."

## Step 4: Essay Structure (2,000 words max)

### Recommended Structure

**Introduction (150–200 words)**
- Hook: striking fact, paradox, or question
- Context: why this question matters now
- Thesis: your clear, arguable position
- Roadmap: briefly signal your 3 main arguments

**Body Paragraph 1 — Your Strongest Argument (350–400 words)**
- Topic sentence stating the argument
- Evidence (academic source + data)
- Analysis: explain WHY this supports your thesis
- Mini-conclusion linking back to thesis

**Body Paragraph 2 — Second Argument (350–400 words)**
- Same structure
- Different type of evidence (e.g., historical example if BP1 used data)

**Body Paragraph 3 — Address the Counterargument (350–400 words)**
- State the strongest objection to your thesis fairly
- Concede what is valid in it
- Rebut: explain why your thesis still holds despite this objection
- This is where most students lose marks — do this well

**Conclusion (150–200 words)**
- Restate thesis (in different words)
- Summarise your 3 arguments briefly
- Broader implication: why does this matter beyond the essay?
- End with a memorable final sentence

## Step 5: Citation Format (MLA 8)

### Book
> Last, First. *Title of Book*. Publisher, Year.
> Example: Müller, Jan-Werner. *What Is Populism?* University of Pennsylvania Press, 2016.

### Journal Article
> Last, First. "Title of Article." *Journal Name*, vol. #, no. #, Year, pp. #–#.

### Website
> Last, First. "Title of Page." *Website Name*, Day Month Year, URL.

### In-text citation
> (Müller 45) — author surname + page number in parentheses

## Red Flags to Avoid

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do Instead |
|---------|-------------|
| Summarise what others think | Make your own argument |
| Use AI to write any part | Use AI only to find sources, then read them yourself |
| Pick the most popular prompt | Pick the prompt where you can be most original |
| Ignore the counterargument | Dedicate a full paragraph to it |
| Exceed 2,000 words | Cut ruthlessly — every sentence must earn its place |
| Use Wikipedia as a source | Use Wikipedia to find sources, then cite the original |
| Submit with your name in the PDF | Remove all identifying information |
