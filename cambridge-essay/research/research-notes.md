# Research Notes — Cambridge Centre Essay Competition

## Prompt: Is multilateralism still viable in an era of rising nationalism?

---

## Section 1: Evidence That Multilateralism Is Failing

### 1.1 UN Security Council Paralysis
- Russia vetoed 17 UNSC resolutions on Syria (2011–2022)
- Russia vetoed resolutions condemning Ukraine invasion (Feb 2022)
- US vetoed resolutions on Gaza ceasefire (2023–2024)
- China and Russia vetoed North Korea sanctions tightening (2022)
- **Data point:** UNSC vetoes increased from 2 (2010–2015) to 12 (2020–2024)
- **Conclusion:** The P5 veto system makes UNSC ineffective on great power conflicts

### 1.2 WTO Dysfunction
- Doha Development Round launched 2001 — collapsed 2008, never completed
- US blocked Appellate Body appointments from 2017 — body became non-functional 2019
- US-China trade war (2018–present): $360 billion in tariffs imposed outside WTO framework
- **Data point:** WTO dispute settlement cases fell 40% after Appellate Body collapse
- **Conclusion:** The rules-based trading system is being bypassed by great powers

### 1.3 Paris Agreement Gaps
- Non-binding nationally determined contributions (NDCs)
- US withdrew 2017, re-entered 2021, uncertain under Trump 2.0 (2025)
- Current NDCs put world on track for 2.5–2.9°C warming (UNEP 2023)
- **Data point:** Global CO2 emissions hit record high in 2023 (36.8 Gt — IEA)
- **Conclusion:** Voluntary multilateralism insufficient for climate emergency

### 1.4 COVID-19 Response Failures
- WHO delayed declaring PHEIC (Public Health Emergency of International Concern) — January 30, 2020, weeks after evidence of human transmission
- US-China blame game paralysed WHO response
- Vaccine nationalism: rich countries hoarded vaccines; COVAX reached only 20% of target by end of 2021
- **Data point:** High-income countries had 50x more vaccines per capita than low-income countries (Oxfam, 2021)

### 1.5 Rise of Nationalist Governments
- 2016: Brexit referendum (52% Leave), Trump elected
- 2018: Bolsonaro (Brazil), Salvini (Italy)
- 2022: Meloni (Italy), Orbán re-elected (Hungary)
- 2024: Trump re-elected; AfD 20%+ in German elections; Le Pen's RN largest party in French parliament
- **Data point:** V-Dem Institute: 72% of world's population now lives under autocratic or backsliding governments (2023)

## Section 2: Evidence That Multilateralism Persists

### 2.1 Paris Agreement Resilience
- 195 countries submitted NDCs — unprecedented global consensus
- Renewable energy investment: $1.7 trillion in 2023 (IEA) — surpassed fossil fuel investment for first time
- COP28 (Dubai, 2023): First-ever agreement to "transition away from fossil fuels" — 198 countries
- EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) — multilateral climate policy with teeth
- **Conclusion:** Despite gaps, Paris framework is producing real behavioural change

### 2.2 COVAX and Global Health Architecture
- COVAX delivered 2 billion doses to 146 countries by end of 2022
- IHR (International Health Regulations) framework improved pandemic surveillance
- WHO's PAHO coordinated Latin American response effectively
- Pandemic Treaty negotiations ongoing (2024) — 194 WHO member states participating
- **Conclusion:** COVID-19 exposed weaknesses but also demonstrated multilateral capacity

### 2.3 G20 Financial Coordination
- G20 coordinated $12 trillion fiscal stimulus in 2020 — prevented Great Depression-scale collapse
- Common Framework for Debt Treatment (2020) — multilateral debt relief for poorest countries
- G20 global minimum corporate tax agreement (2021): 136 countries, 15% minimum rate
- **Data point:** IMF estimates G20 coordination prevented 12% global GDP loss in 2020
- **Conclusion:** Minilateral financial coordination works even amid geopolitical tensions

### 2.4 Nuclear Non-Proliferation
- NPT (Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty): 191 state parties — most widely adhered arms control treaty
- Despite North Korea and Iran challenges, no new nuclear state since 1998 (Pakistan)
- New START treaty (US-Russia) maintained until 2026 despite Ukraine war
- **Conclusion:** Nuclear multilateralism has largely held despite great power tensions

### 2.5 UNGA Consensus on Ukraine
- UNGA Resolution ES-11/1 (March 2022): 141 countries condemned Russian invasion
- Only 5 countries voted against (Russia, Belarus, North Korea, Eritrea, Syria)
- 35 abstentions (including China, India) — not endorsement of Russia
- **Conclusion:** Multilateral consensus exists outside UNSC; the UN system is not dead

## Section 3: The Adaptive Multilateralism Argument

### 3.1 Minilateralism in Practice
- **QUAD** (US, India, Japan, Australia): Indo-Pacific security, vaccine distribution, tech cooperation
- **AUKUS** (Australia, UK, US): Nuclear submarine technology sharing
- **G7+** coordination on Russia sanctions: unprecedented scope — $300 billion in frozen Russian assets
- **Chip 4 Alliance** (US, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan): Semiconductor supply chain security
- **Naím's argument:** "The smallest number of countries needed to have the largest impact on solving a particular problem"

### 3.2 Regional Multilateralism Strengthening
- **EU:** Despite Brexit, EU expanded to 27 members; accession negotiations with Ukraine, Moldova, Western Balkans
- **ASEAN:** Maintained neutrality and engagement with all great powers; RCEP (2022) — world's largest trade agreement
- **African Union:** AU gained permanent G20 membership (2023); Agenda 2063 framework
- **MERCOSUR-EU FTA:** Negotiations concluded 2024 after 25 years

### 3.3 New Issue-Specific Multilateralism
- **AI Governance:** G7 Hiroshima AI Process (2023); Bletchley Park AI Safety Summit (2023); UN AI Advisory Body (2024)
- **Pandemic Treaty:** WHO negotiations for binding pandemic preparedness agreement
- **High Seas Treaty:** BBNJ Agreement (2023) — first treaty governing international waters
- **Debt:** Common Framework for Debt Treatment; Paris Club coordination

### 3.4 Global South Demanding Reformed Multilateralism
- India's G20 Presidency (2023): "Voice of the Global South" summit; African Union G20 membership
- BRICS expansion (2024): Not anti-multilateral but demanding multipolar multilateralism
- "Reformist multilateralism" — not abandoning institutions but demanding representation
- **Key insight:** The Global South's critique is of *Western-dominated* multilateralism, not multilateralism per se

## Section 6: Potential Essay Hooks

1. **The paradox hook:** "In December 2023, 198 nations agreed in Dubai to transition away from fossil fuels — the most ambitious climate commitment in history. Three months earlier, the UN Security Council had failed for the 17th time to pass a resolution on Syria. Multilateralism is simultaneously dying and thriving."

2. **The historical hook:** "When the League of Nations collapsed in 1939, it seemed to prove that multilateralism was a utopian fantasy. Yet within six years, the United Nations, IMF, World Bank, and GATT had been created. Today's crisis of multilateralism may be less an ending than a transformation."

3. **The data hook:** "141 countries — representing 80% of the world's population — voted to condemn Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Yet the UN Security Council, the body designed to enforce such consensus, was paralysed by a single veto. This contradiction defines the multilateral moment."
