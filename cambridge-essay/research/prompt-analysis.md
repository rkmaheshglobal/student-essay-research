# Prompt Deep Analysis — Cambridge Centre Essay Competition

## The Prompt

> "In an era of rising nationalism and geopolitical fragmentation, is multilateralism still a viable framework for addressing global challenges?"

---

## Term-by-Term Deconstruction

### "Era of rising nationalism"
- **What it assumes:** Nationalism is measurably rising — this is a premise you can accept, qualify, or challenge
- **Evidence for:** Brexit (2016), Trump's "America First" (2017–2021, 2025–), Modi's Hindu nationalism, Orbán's Hungary, Le Pen's France, Bolsonaro's Brazil, AfD in Germany
- **Nuance:** Nationalism ≠ isolationism. Some nationalist governments still engage multilaterally when it serves interests (e.g., India in G20, BRICS)
- **Key question:** Is this a permanent structural shift or a cyclical backlash?

### "Geopolitical fragmentation"
- **What it means:** The breakdown of the post-1945 liberal international order into competing blocs
- **Evidence:** US-China decoupling, Russia-West rupture post-Ukraine, Global South asserting independence, BRICS expansion (2024: Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iran, Ethiopia, Egypt, Argentina invited)
- **Key question:** Is fragmentation the same as the end of multilateralism, or just a reconfiguration?

### "Multilateralism"
- **Definition:** A principle of coordinating national policies among three or more states on the basis of generalised principles of conduct (Ruggie, 1992)
- **Types to distinguish:**
  - **Universal multilateralism:** UN, WTO, WHO — all states, binding rules
  - **Minilateralism:** G7, G20, QUAD, AUKUS — small groups of powerful states
  - **Regional multilateralism:** EU, ASEAN, African Union — geographically bounded
  - **Issue-specific multilateralism:** Paris Agreement, NPT, COVAX
- **Critical insight:** The prompt says "multilateralism" — but which kind? This distinction is your analytical edge

### "Viable framework"
- **Viable ≠ perfect.** The question is whether it *can work*, not whether it *does work optimally*
- **Viable means:** Capable of producing binding agreements, enforcement mechanisms, and actual behavioural change among states
- **The bar:** Compare to alternatives — bilateral deals, unilateral action, regional blocs. Is multilateralism better than these alternatives?

### "Global challenges"
- **Which challenges?** Climate change, pandemic preparedness, nuclear proliferation, AI governance, trade, migration, debt
- **Key insight:** Multilateralism may be viable for some challenges (climate) but not others (great power competition). A nuanced essay distinguishes between issue areas

## Argument Frameworks

### Framework 1: Realist Critique
**Claim:** Multilateralism was always a reflection of US hegemony. As US power declines and China rises, the institutional architecture built on American power loses its enforcer.
- Mearsheimer: States pursue relative gains, not absolute gains — multilateral cooperation is inherently unstable
- Waltz: Structural anarchy means states will defect when interests diverge
- Evidence: WTO dispute settlement paralysis (US blocking Appellate Body appointments since 2017), UN Security Council deadlock on Ukraine/Gaza

### Framework 2: Liberal Institutionalist Defence
**Claim:** Institutions create path dependencies, reduce transaction costs, and build habits of cooperation that survive hegemonic decline.
- Keohane: "After Hegemony" (1984) — institutions persist even when the hegemon weakens
- Ikenberry: The liberal order is "sticky" — states have invested too much to abandon it
- Evidence: Paris Agreement survived US withdrawal and re-entry; WHO coordinated COVID-19 response despite US-China tensions; WTO still processes 95% of trade disputes

### Framework 3: Adaptive Multilateralism
**Claim:** The form of multilateralism is changing, not dying. Minilateral coalitions and issue-specific agreements are more effective than universal institutions.
- Naím (2009): "Minilateralism" — the smallest number of countries needed to have the largest impact
- Evidence: QUAD (US, India, Japan, Australia) on Indo-Pacific security; G20 on financial stability; COVAX on vaccine distribution; CHIPS Act + allied semiconductor controls

### Framework 4: Global South Perspective
**Claim:** The crisis of multilateralism is partly a crisis of Western-dominated multilateralism. The Global South is not abandoning multilateralism — it's demanding reformed multilateralism.
- BRICS expansion as alternative multilateralism, not anti-multilateralism
- African Union's demand for permanent UNSC seat
- India's G20 presidency (2023): "One Earth, One Family, One Future"
- Evidence: 141 countries voted to condemn Russia's invasion (UNGA) — multilateral consensus still exists outside UNSC

## The Strongest Counterarguments (Must Address)

### Against multilateralism:
1. **Climate:** Paris Agreement is non-binding; emissions still rising; COP process produces pledges not action
2. **Trade:** WTO Doha Round collapsed; US-China trade war bypasses WTO; bilateral FTAs proliferating
3. **Security:** UN Security Council paralysed by P5 veto; NATO expansion triggered Russian aggression
4. **Health:** WHO failed to declare COVID-19 emergency quickly; US-China blame game undermined response
5. **AI:** No multilateral AI governance framework exists; states racing to develop AI unilaterally

### For multilateralism (rebuttals):
1. **Climate:** Despite non-binding nature, 195 countries submitted NDCs; renewable energy investment surged post-Paris
2. **Trade:** WTO still governs $28 trillion in annual trade; bilateral deals use WTO as baseline
3. **Security:** NATO deterred Russian aggression in NATO territory; multilateral sanctions on Russia unprecedented in scope
4. **Health:** COVAX delivered 2 billion doses; IHR framework improved pandemic surveillance
5. **AI:** G7 Hiroshima AI Process (2023); Bletchley Park AI Safety Summit (2023) — multilateral AI governance emerging

## Winning Essay Structure (2,000 words)

### Introduction (200 words)
- Hook: A striking example of multilateralism failing AND succeeding simultaneously (e.g., COP28 reached fossil fuel deal despite geopolitical tensions)
- Define key terms: multilateralism, nationalism, geopolitical fragmentation
- Thesis: State your position clearly — conditional viability is the most defensible

### Body Paragraph 1 — The Case Against (400 words)
- Strongest evidence that multilateralism is failing
- UNSC paralysis, WTO dysfunction, Paris Agreement gaps
- Cite: Mearsheimer, Waltz, concrete data

### Body Paragraph 2 — The Case For (400 words)
- Evidence that multilateralism persists and delivers
- Paris Agreement, COVAX, G20 financial coordination
- Cite: Keohane, Ikenberry, concrete data

### Body Paragraph 3 — The Nuanced Argument (400 words)
- Your original contribution: distinguish between issue areas, types of multilateralism
- Minilateralism as evolution, not failure
- Global South demanding reformed multilateralism, not abandoning it

### Conclusion (200 words)
- Restate thesis with nuance
- Avoid simple yes/no — "viable but transformed"
- End with a forward-looking observation
