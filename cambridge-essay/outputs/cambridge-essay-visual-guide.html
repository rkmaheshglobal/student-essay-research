Prompt 1 • Politics / Democracy • Contributed by Harvard Professor
Is populism inevitably a destructive force in democracy, or can it be reimagined as a positive force for change?
High CompetitionClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The core tension: populism exposes real elite corruption but often slides into authoritarianism. Your angle: distinguish between form (people vs. elites narrative) and content (exclusionary vs. inclusive). Argue that populism is a symptom, not a cause.

📚 Key Books

Müller: What Is Populism? (2016) • Levitsky & Ziblatt: How Democracies Die (2018) • Mudde & Kaltwasser: Populism: A Very Short Introduction (2017)

Prompt 2 • IR / Political Theory • Contributed by Stanford Professor
Can the nation-state survive the pressures of globalisation, climate change, and forced migration?
Broad ScopeClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

Three pressures identified by the professor: (1) digital flows states can't control, (2) climate costs, (3) forced migration. The rightward tilt is a reaction that may accelerate state decline. Your angle: distinguish between state form and state function — functions may survive even if forms change.

📚 Key Books

Anderson: Imagined Communities (1983) • Appadurai: Modernity at Large (1996) • Hobsbawm: Nations and Nationalism Since 1780 (1990)

Prompt 3 • AI / Law / Ethics • Contributed by Berkeley Professor
Should AI systems have legal personhood?
Highly TopicalClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The professor's framing: legal personhood requires answering whether AI can be sued, can live independently, and can exist outside ownership. Current answer: no. Your angle: argue that the real question is not personhood but accountability — enhanced corporate liability is more effective than personhood.

📚 Key Books

Mitchell: AI: A Guide for Thinking Humans (2019) • Gunkel: The Machine Question (2012) • Zuboff: The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (2019)

Prompt 4 • History / Ethics • Contributed by Cornell Professor
Should colonial-era artifacts in Western museums be repatriated to their countries of origin, or preserved where they are as part of global heritage?
Rich LiteratureClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

Two sides: universal museum argument (artifacts belong to all humanity; Western museums have preservation capacity) vs. repatriation argument (colonial acquisition was non-consensual; modern technology solves preservation concerns). Your angle: the consent question is decisive — acquisition without consent cannot be legitimised by subsequent stewardship.

📚 Key Books

Hicks: The Brutish Museums (2020) • Cuno: Who Owns Antiquity? (2008) • Said: Culture and Imperialism (1993)

Prompt 5 • Sociology / Economics • Contributed by UPenn Professor
Can meritocracy and structural inequality coexist, or does one inevitably undermine the other?
NuancedClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The professor's framing: definitions matter — what kind of meritocracy? What kind of structural inequality? Your angle: formal meritocracy (equal rules) can coexist with structural inequality, but substantive meritocracy (equal outcomes of effort) cannot — because structural inequality determines who can compete.

📚 Key Books

Sandel: The Tyranny of Merit (2020) • Young: The Rise of the Meritocracy (1958) • Rawls: A Theory of Justice (1971)

Prompt 6 • Ethics / Migration • Contributed by Princeton Professor
Are open borders morally justified?
Clear FrameworkClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The professor's framing: borders are moral boundaries that determine who gets safety and opportunity. Evidence shows immigrants strengthen economies. Your angle: open borders are morally justified as an extension of freedom of movement, but the argument must address the social trust objection (Miller) directly.

📚 Key Books

Carens: The Ethics of Immigration (2013) • Miller: Strangers in Our Midst (2016) • Arendt: The Origins of Totalitarianism (1951)

Prompt 7 • Philosophy of Science • Contributed by Oxford Professor
Should the pursuit of scientific knowledge be constrained by ethical considerations?
Classic DebateClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The tension: scientific freedom vs. ethical responsibility. Your angle: the question is not whether but how — distinguish between restricting research (problematic) and restricting application (necessary). Use genome editing as the case study.

📚 Key Books

Jasanoff: The Ethics of Invention (2016) • Mazzucato: The Entrepreneurial State (2013) • Feynman: The Pleasure of Finding Things Out (1999)

Prompt 8 • Bioethics / Technology • Contributed by Columbia Professor
Should some technologies be restricted even if they could eliminate disease or extend human life?
High StakesClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The professor's example: genome editing in human embryos — could eliminate Huntington's disease but risks are irreversible. Your angle: the irreversibility criterion is key — technologies that permanently alter the human gene pool or social fabric require a higher burden of proof before deployment.

📚 Key Books

Bostrom: Superintelligence (2014) • Harari: Homo Deus (2016) • Shelley: Frankenstein (1818)

Prompt 9 • Philosophy / Medicine / Culture • Contributed by Cambridge Professor
What does it mean to heal?
Most OriginalClick to expand
💡 Strategic Analysis

The professor's framing: healing comes from Old English hælan — to make whole. Modern tension: quick-fix wellness culture vs. deep understanding of trauma and rehabilitation. Your angle: healing is not restoration of a prior state but integration of loss — a social and cultural process, not merely a medical one.

📚 Key Books

Frankl: Man's Search for Meaning (1946) • van der Kolk: The Body Keeps the Score (2014) • Sontag: Illness as Metaphor (1978)

🎯 Prompt Selection Strategy
How to Choose Your Prompt
  • 1Genuine interest — You will spend weeks on this. Pick something you actually want to think about.
  • 2Original angle — Can you say something the obvious essay won't say? If not, pick a different prompt.
  • 3Research access — Can you access 3–5 academic books or articles? Check your school library first.
  • 4Avoid the crowd — Prompts 1 (Populism) and 2 (Nation-states) will attract the most submissions. Harder to stand out.

💡 What Makes a Winning Essay
✅ Winning Essays Do This
  • ✓Take a clear, specific, arguable thesis in the introduction
  • ✓Use academic sources from the professor reading lists
  • ✓Dedicate a full paragraph to the strongest counterargument
  • ✓Distinguish between types or cases (not all X is the same)
  • ✓End with a broader implication, not just a summary
  • ✓Cite every claim in MLA 8 format
❌ Losing Essays Do This
  • ✗Summarise what others think without taking a position
  • ✗Use Wikipedia or news articles as primary sources
  • ✗Ignore the counterargument entirely
  • ✗Write a vague thesis like "this is a complex issue"
  • ✗Exceed 2,000 words
  • ✗Use AI to write any part of the essay
📝 Sample Thesis Statements
Prompt 3 — AI Legal Personhood
"Although AI systems increasingly exhibit human-like reasoning, granting them legal personhood would create accountability gaps that undermine rather than strengthen human oversight — a more effective approach is enhanced corporate liability for AI developers."
Prompt 4 — Colonial Artifacts
"While universal museum arguments have merit, the case for repatriation is stronger: colonial acquisition was rarely consensual, and modern preservation technology eliminates the practical objections to return."
Prompt 6 — Open Borders
"Open borders are morally justified as an extension of the basic human right to freedom of movement, but their implementation requires phased institutional reform — the moral case is clear even if the political path is not."
Prompt 9 — What Does It Mean to Heal?
"Healing is not the restoration of a prior state but the creation of a new relationship with loss — a process that is irreducibly social and cultural, not merely medical, and that requires community as much as treatment."
📄 Essay Structure (2,000 Words)
💡 This structure works for all 9 prompts. Adapt the word counts slightly based on your argument complexity.
📄 Recommended Word Budget
SectionWordsPurposeKey Elements
Introduction150–200Hook, context, thesis, roadmapStart with a striking fact or paradox; state thesis clearly in last sentence
Body 1 — Strongest Argument350–400Your best evidence for the thesisTopic sentence + academic source + data + analysis + link to thesis
Body 2 — Second Argument350–400Different type of evidenceUse a different evidence type (e.g., historical example if BP1 used data)
Body 3 — Counterargument350–400Address the strongest objectionState it fairly → concede what is valid → rebut why thesis still holds
Conclusion150–200Synthesis + broader implicationRestate thesis (different words) + summarise + end with a memorable sentence
🔎 Introduction Formula
The 4-Sentence Introduction
  • 1Hook — A striking statistic, paradox, or concrete example that makes the reader want to continue
  • 2Context — Why does this question matter right now? What is at stake?
  • 3Tension — Briefly acknowledge the complexity (the two sides of the debate)
  • 4Thesis — Your clear, arguable position. This is the most important sentence in the essay.
💡 Sample Hooks by Prompt
Prompt 1 — Populism

"In 2024, for the first time in American history, a convicted felon won the presidency with 77 million votes. Whether this represents democracy working or democracy failing depends entirely on how one defines populism."

Prompt 3 — AI Legal Personhood

"In 2023, a Colombian judge cited ChatGPT in a legal ruling. In 2024, an AI system was listed as a co-inventor on a patent application. The question of whether AI should have legal personhood is no longer theoretical."

Prompt 4 — Colonial Artifacts

"The Benin Bronzes were looted from Nigeria in 1897 by British soldiers who described the raid as a 'punitive expedition.' Today, 900 of them sit in the British Museum. The question of who owns them is also a question of who owns history."

Prompt 6 — Open Borders

"Every year, thousands of people die attempting to cross borders that their grandparents crossed freely. The question of whether borders are morally justified is also a question of whether birthplace should determine destiny."

Prompt 9 — What Does It Mean to Heal?

"The English word 'heal' comes from the Old English hælan — to make whole. But what does wholeness mean after loss? This question, which medicine has largely abandoned to philosophy, may be the most important question of our time."

📚 Reading Strategy
  • 1Read the introduction and conclusion of each book first — get the argument
  • 2Find the chapter most relevant to your thesis and read it fully
  • 3Note key quotes with page numbers as you read
  • 4Look at the bibliography of each book for more sources
  • 5Aim for 5–8 sources minimum; quality over quantity
📅 8-Week Writing Timeline
Now — Week 1
Choose Your Prompt
Score each prompt on interest, knowledge, and originality potential. Commit to one.
Week 1–2
Deep Research
Read 3–5 books from the professor reading list. Take notes with page numbers. Find 2–3 key data points.
Week 2
Develop Your Thesis
Write 5 possible thesis statements. Choose the most specific and arguable one.
Week 3
Write First Draft
Aim for 1,800 words. Don't edit as you write — just get the argument down.
Week 4
Peer Review
Share with a teacher or trusted peer. Ask: Is the thesis clear? Is the counterargument addressed?
Week 5
Revise Argument
Strengthen weak paragraphs. Add missing evidence. Cut anything that doesn't support the thesis.
Week 6
Polish Writing
Read aloud to catch awkward sentences. Vary sentence length. Check vocabulary precision.
Week 7
Citations & Format
Check every citation is in MLA 8. Format PDF. Remove your name and school from the document.
Submission
Submit via CCIR Portal
Double-check word count, PDF format, blind review compliance, and referee email.

✅ Final Submission Checklist
🚫 The competition uses both a plagiarism checker AND an AI checker. Any AI-assisted writing will be detected and disqualified.
📄 Before You Write
  • ☐Chosen ONE prompt only
  • ☐Read at least 3 books from the professor reading list
  • ☐Written a clear, arguable thesis statement
  • ☐Outlined your 3 body paragraphs
  • ☐Identified the strongest counterargument to address
✍️ While Writing
  • ☐Every claim is supported by an academic source
  • ☐Counterargument is addressed fairly and rebutted
  • ☐Introduction has a hook, context, and clear thesis
  • ☐Conclusion goes beyond summary to broader implication
  • ☐Writing is entirely your own (no AI assistance)
📄 Before Submission
  • ☐Word count is 2,000 or fewer (excluding footnotes & bibliography)
  • ☐All citations are in MLA 8 format
  • ☐Essay is in PDF format
  • ☐Name, school, and personal info REMOVED from PDF
  • ☐Referee email address ready (teacher or counsellor)
  • ☐Submitted via CCIR portal before deadline
🚫 Never Do This
  • !Use AI to write, paraphrase, or edit any part of the essay
  • !Copy text from any source without citation
  • !Ask someone else to write or co-write the essay
  • !Submit more than one essay
  • !Leave your name in the PDF document
