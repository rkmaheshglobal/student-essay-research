### Question Structure Patterns

**Pattern 1: "Should we/governments [policy action]?"**
- Direct policy questions requiring yes/no with nuance
- Examples: "Should we tax carbon?", "Should governments pick winners?"
- Frequency: 50% of questions

**Pattern 2: "Is [X] always/ever [Y]?"**
- Challenges absolute statements
- Examples: "Is inflation always bad?", "Is competition always good?"
- Frequency: 22% of questions

**Pattern 3: "Is [X] compatible with/good for [Y]?"**
- Examines relationships between concepts
- Examples: "Is capitalism compatible with climate change?", "Is globalization good for the poor?"
- Frequency: 17% of questions

**Pattern 4: "Is [X] necessary for [Y]?"**
- Causal relationship questions
- Examples: "Is inequality necessary for growth?"
- Frequency: 11% of questions

## Winning Strategies by Question Type

### Strategy 1: Policy Evaluation Questions

**Question Pattern:** "Should we/governments [do X]?"

**Winning Approach:**
1. **Don't give a simple yes/no** - The best answers are conditional
2. **Framework:** "It depends on [conditions A, B, C]"
3. **Structure:**
   - Define the policy precisely
   - Identify the goals (efficiency, equity, sustainability)
   - Evaluate against each goal
   - Consider implementation challenges
   - Conclude with conditional recommendation

**Example Application:** "Should governments tax wealth?"

*Weak answer:* "Yes, because inequality is bad" or "No, because it's inefficient"

*Strong answer:* "The case for wealth taxation depends on three factors: the design of the tax (annual vs. one-time), the existing tax structure (whether income and capital gains are already taxed), and the institutional capacity to enforce it. In countries with weak income taxation and high inequality, a well-designed wealth tax can improve both efficiency and equity. In countries with comprehensive income taxation, the case is weaker."

---

### Strategy 2: Assumption Challenge Questions

**Question Pattern:** "Is [X] always/ever [Y]?"

**Winning Approach:**
1. **The answer is almost always "no, not always"** - Find the exceptions
2. **Framework:** Identify conditions under which the assumption holds and fails
3. **Structure:**
   - Explain why the assumption seems intuitive
   - Provide counterexamples where it fails
   - Identify the conditions that determine outcomes
   - Conclude with a more nuanced principle

**Example Application:** "Is inflation always bad?"

*Weak answer:* "No, because moderate inflation is fine"

*Strong answer:* "Inflation's effects depend on who you are and what you own. For debtors, moderate inflation erodes the real value of debt—a transfer from creditors to borrowers. For workers with wage bargaining power, inflation can be neutral. For savers on fixed incomes, inflation is unambiguously harmful. The question isn't whether inflation is 'bad' but for whom, at what level, and compared to what alternative. Deflation, for instance, can be far more destructive."

---

### Strategy 3: Systemic/Conceptual Questions

**Question Pattern:** "Is [system/concept X] compatible with [goal Y]?"

**Winning Approach:**
1. **Define both terms precisely** - What do we mean by "capitalism"? What counts as "addressing climate change"?
2. **Framework:** Distinguish between different versions of X and different interpretations of Y
3. **Structure:**
   - Define key terms (acknowledge multiple definitions)
   - Identify tensions between X and Y
   - Identify potential compatibilities
   - Argue for a specific version of X that could achieve Y
   - Acknowledge what would need to change

**Example Application:** "Is capitalism compatible with addressing climate change?"

*Weak answer:* "No, because capitalism requires growth" or "Yes, because markets can solve it"

*Strong answer:* "The answer depends on what we mean by 'capitalism.' If capitalism means an economic system driven by profit maximization with externalities unpriced, then no—the incentive structure is fundamentally misaligned with climate stability. But if capitalism means a system with private ownership, markets, and prices that can be shaped by policy, then compatibility is possible through carbon pricing, regulation, and public investment. The question is whether the political economy of capitalism allows such reforms, or whether the power of fossil fuel interests makes them impossible."

---

### Strategy 4: Distributional Questions

**Question Pattern:** "Is [X] good for [group Y]?" or "Is [X] fair?"

**Winning Approach:**
1. **Distinguish absolute from relative effects** - Better off than before vs. better off than others
2. **Framework:** Who gains, who loses, by how much, and is compensation possible?
3. **Structure:**
   - Define "good for" or "fair" (multiple interpretations)
   - Identify winners and losers
   - Quantify effects where possible
   - Consider dynamic effects (short-term vs. long-term)
   - Evaluate fairness of process vs. outcomes

**Example Application:** "Is globalization good for the poor?"

*Weak answer:* "Yes, because it lifted millions out of poverty" or "No, because it increased inequality"

*Strong answer:* "Globalization has lifted hundreds of millions out of extreme poverty in China and East Asia—an unprecedented achievement. But 'the poor' are not a monolith. Factory workers in Vietnam gained; factory workers in Ohio lost. The global poor gained in absolute terms; the working class in rich countries lost in relative terms. Whether globalization is 'good for the poor' depends on which poor, over what time horizon, and whether the gains could have been achieved through alternative policies with fewer losers."

## 2025 Questions: Detailed Analysis

### Question 1: "Should AI-generated content be taxed?"

**Question Type:** Policy Evaluation

**Why This Question Was Asked:**
- AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney) exploded in 2023-2024
- Concerns about job displacement in creative industries
- Questions about value creation and economic contribution
- Follows the automation theme from 2021

**The Obvious Arguments:**

*For Taxation:*
- AI displaces human workers who would pay income tax
- AI companies capture value without proportional tax contribution
- Revenue could fund retraining programs or UBI
- Creates level playing field between human and AI labor

*Against Taxation:*
- Difficult to define and measure "AI-generated content"
- Could stifle innovation and productivity gains
- Administrative complexity and enforcement challenges
- May drive AI development offshore

**Winning Approach:**

*Thesis:* "The question is not whether to tax AI content, but how to ensure AI's productivity gains are broadly shared. Taxing outputs is impractical; taxing the windfall profits of AI companies and redistributing through education and social insurance is more effective."

*Original Angle:* Frame this as a question about the social contract in the age of AI. The issue isn't the content—it's the distribution of gains from automation.

**Key Data Points:**
- Goldman Sachs: AI could automate 300M jobs globally
- OpenAI revenue: $2B+ annually by 2025
- Creative industry employment: 5% of US workforce
- Historical precedent: No country successfully taxes automation directly

### Question 2: "Is degrowth the answer to climate change?"

**Question Type:** Systemic Evaluation

**Why This Question Was Asked:**
- Follows 2023's "Is growth a good measure of progress?"
- COP28 and ongoing climate negotiations
- Growing academic interest in degrowth (Hickel, Kallis)
- Tension between development and sustainability

**The Obvious Arguments:**

*For Degrowth:*
- No country has achieved absolute decoupling at scale
- Growth requires energy; energy causes emissions
- Rich countries have already developed; they can afford to shrink
- Consumerism drives environmental destruction

*Against Degrowth:*
- Degrowth means poverty for billions
- Technology can enable green growth
- Political impossibility—no one votes for less
- Developing countries need growth to escape poverty

**Winning Approach:**

*Thesis:* "Degrowth is the wrong frame. The question is not 'more or less GDP' but 'growth of what?' We need selective growth—more healthcare, education, and clean energy; less fossil fuels, fast fashion, and planned obsolescence."

*Original Angle:* Reject the binary. Argue for "post-growth" thinking that focuses on what we want to grow and what we want to shrink, rather than aggregate GDP.

**Key Data Points:**
- No country has achieved absolute decoupling (EEA)
- 1.5°C requires 7.6% annual emission cuts (UNEP)
- Global South needs $2.4T/year for climate transition
- Bhutan: carbon-negative country with Gross National Happiness

---

### Question 3: "Should central banks issue digital currencies?"

**Question Type:** Policy Evaluation

**Why This Question Was Asked:**
- China's digital yuan has 260M+ users
- ECB, Fed, Bank of England all exploring CBDCs
- Follows 2023's "Should central banks be independent?"
- Cryptocurrency and stablecoin debates

**The Obvious Arguments:**

*For CBDCs:*
- Financial inclusion for 1.4B unbanked adults
- More efficient payment systems
- Better monetary policy transmission
- Reduce shadow economy and tax evasion

*Against CBDCs:*
- Privacy concerns—government tracks all transactions
- Disintermediation of commercial banks
- Cybersecurity risks
- Programmable money enables authoritarian control

**Winning Approach:**

*Thesis:* "The question is not whether CBDCs should exist, but how they should be designed. A privacy-preserving, token-based CBDC could enhance financial inclusion without enabling surveillance. A fully traceable, account-based CBDC would be a tool of control."

*Original Angle:* Focus on design choices. The same technology can serve freedom or oppression—it depends on implementation.

**Key Data Points:**
- 130+ countries exploring CBDCs (Atlantic Council)
- China's e-CNY: 260M users, $250B in transactions
- 1.4B adults globally without bank accounts
- Sweden: cash use fell from 40% to 9% (2010-2020)

## How to Prepare for Any Question

### The Universal Framework

Regardless of the specific question, every winning essay should:

1. **Define key terms** (first 10% of essay)
   - Acknowledge that terms have multiple meanings
   - State which definition you're using and why

2. **Present the strongest case for the obvious answer** (next 20%)
   - Show you understand the conventional wisdom
   - Use evidence to support it

3. **Complicate the picture** (next 30%)
   - Introduce counterexamples, exceptions, or overlooked factors
   - Challenge assumptions in the question itself

4. **Develop your original argument** (next 25%)
   - This is where you differentiate yourself
   - Offer a framework or perspective others won't have

5. **Conclude with nuance** (final 15%)
   - Avoid simple yes/no
   - Specify conditions under which your answer holds
   - Acknowledge limitations

### The "It Depends" Framework

For almost any economics question, the winning answer involves:

| Factor | Question to Ask |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Time horizon** | Short-term vs. long-term effects? |
| **Scale** | Does it work at small scale but not large? |
| **Context** | Does it depend on institutions, culture, or history? |
| **Distribution** | Who gains and who loses? |
| **Alternatives** | Compared to what? |
| **Implementation** | Does theory match practice? |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Taking the Question at Face Value
- **Problem:** Answering "Should we tax carbon?" with just "yes" or "no"
- **Solution:** Question the question—what kind of carbon tax? At what level? With what revenue use?

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Counterarguments
- **Problem:** Only presenting evidence for your position
- **Solution:** Dedicate 20-25% of essay to the strongest objections and your responses

### Mistake 3: Being Too Abstract
- **Problem:** Discussing "inequality" without specific examples
- **Solution:** Use concrete cases—"In the UK, the Gini coefficient rose from 0.25 to 0.34 between 1979 and 2019"

### Mistake 4: Moralizing Without Analysis
- **Problem:** "Inequality is wrong because it's unfair"
- **Solution:** Explain *why* it might be wrong—reduced social mobility, political capture, demand deficiency

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Trade-offs
- **Problem:** Presenting your preferred policy as costless
- **Solution:** Acknowledge costs and explain why benefits outweigh them

## Practice Exercise

Take any past question and practice the following:

1. **Identify the question type** (policy, assumption challenge, systemic, distributional)
2. **List 3 arguments for the "obvious" answer**
3. **List 3 counterarguments or complications**
4. **Develop your "original angle"**
5. **Write a thesis statement that captures nuance**

### Example: "Is competition always good?" (2022)

1. **Type:** Assumption challenge

2. **Arguments for "yes, competition is good":**
   - Drives efficiency and innovation
   - Lowers prices for consumers
   - Prevents monopoly abuse

3. **Counterarguments/complications:**
   - Natural monopolies (utilities, infrastructure)
   - Race to the bottom (labor standards, environment)
   - Winner-take-all markets (tech platforms)
   - Destructive competition (price wars that destroy value)

4. **Original angle:** Competition is a means, not an end. The goal is welfare, and sometimes cooperation or regulation achieves it better.

5. **Thesis:** "Competition is a powerful tool for achieving efficiency and innovation, but it is not an end in itself. In markets with network effects, natural monopolies, or significant externalities, competition can be destructive rather than creative. The question is not whether competition is 'good' but under what conditions it serves human welfare—and when alternative arrangements might serve it better."

5. **Original angles differentiate** - Find the perspective others won't have
