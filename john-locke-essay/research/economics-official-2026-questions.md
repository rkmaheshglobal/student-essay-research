# QUESTION 1: Should we fear a cashless society?

## Question Analysis

**Question Type:** Evaluation/Fear Assessment

**Key Terms to Define:**
- "Fear" - What constitutes a legitimate concern vs. irrational fear?
- "Cashless society" - Complete elimination of physical currency, or just dominance of digital payments?
- "Society" - Whose society? Developed vs. developing countries?

**What the Question is Really Asking:**
- What are the risks and benefits of eliminating physical cash?
- Are the risks serious enough to warrant "fear"?
- Who gains and who loses from cashlessness?

---

## The Core Debate

### Arguments FOR Fearing a Cashless Society

**1. Privacy and Surveillance**
- Every transaction becomes traceable
- Governments and corporations can monitor all spending
- Potential for authoritarian control (China's social credit system)
- No anonymous transactions possible

**2. Financial Exclusion**
- Elderly, disabled, and technologically illiterate excluded
- Unbanked populations (1.4 billion globally) locked out
- Rural areas with poor connectivity disadvantaged
- Homeless and marginalized groups cannot participate

**3. System Vulnerability**
- Cyberattacks could paralyze entire economy
- Power outages render all transactions impossible
- Technical failures have no backup
- Single points of failure in payment infrastructure

**4. Loss of Monetary Freedom**
- Negative interest rates become enforceable (can't withdraw cash)
- Programmable money could restrict purchases
- Bank runs become impossible (good for banks, bad for depositors)
- Government can freeze accounts instantly

### Arguments AGAINST Fearing a Cashless Society

**1. Efficiency and Convenience**
- Faster transactions, lower costs
- Reduced tax evasion and shadow economy
- Less crime (cash is untraceable)
- Easier cross-border payments

**2. Financial Inclusion (Paradoxically)**
- Mobile money has banked millions in Africa (M-Pesa)
- Digital identity enables access to financial services
- Lower barriers than traditional banking
- Microloans and micropayments become feasible

**3. Hygiene and Safety**
- Cash carries bacteria and viruses (COVID highlighted this)
- Reduced robbery and theft
- No need to carry physical currency
- Counterfeit elimination

**4. Monetary Policy Effectiveness**
- Central banks can implement negative rates if needed
- Better transmission of monetary policy
- Real-time economic data for policymakers
- Reduced cash handling costs for businesses

---

## Winning Argument Framework

### Thesis Option 1 (Nuanced Fear)
*"We should fear not cashlessness itself, but a cashless society designed without safeguards for privacy, inclusion, and resilience. The question is not whether to go cashless, but how to design digital payment systems that preserve the freedoms cash currently provides."*

### Thesis Option 2 (Conditional)
*"Whether to fear a cashless society depends on who designs it and for what purpose. A cashless society designed by authoritarian governments is terrifying; one designed with privacy-preserving technology and universal access could be liberating. The technology is neutral; the institutions are not."*

### Thesis Option 3 (Distributional)
*"The cashless society is already here for the wealthy and connected; the question is whether it will include or exclude the vulnerable. Fear is warranted not for the technology, but for the likelihood that its benefits will accrue to the powerful while its costs fall on the marginalized."*

---

## Recommended Essay Structure

```
I. Introduction (10%)
   - Hook: Sweden is 98% cashless; India demonetized overnight
   - Define "cashless society" and "fear"
   - Thesis: Fear is warranted, but conditional on design choices

II. The Case for Fear (25%)
   - Privacy and surveillance risks
   - Financial exclusion of vulnerable groups
   - System fragility and single points of failure
   - Use specific examples (China, Sweden, India)

III. The Case Against Fear (20%)
   - Efficiency gains and crime reduction
   - Mobile money success stories (M-Pesa)
   - Monetary policy effectiveness
   - Acknowledge these are real benefits

IV. The Real Question: Design Matters (25%)
   - Privacy-preserving digital currencies are possible
   - Inclusion requires deliberate policy
   - Resilience requires redundancy
   - Compare Sweden (inclusive) vs. China (surveillance)

V. Conclusion (20%)
   - Fear is rational given current trajectories
   - But fear should motivate better design, not rejection
   - The cashless society is coming; the question is what kind
```

## Original Angles to Explore

1. **Cash as a Human Right:** Argue that access to anonymous transactions is a fundamental freedom
2. **The Generational Divide:** Frame as conflict between digital natives and elderly
3. **Monetary Sovereignty:** Small countries lose control when dominated by foreign digital payments
4. **The Panopticon Economy:** Reference Bentham/Foucault on surveillance and behavior modification
5. **Resilience vs. Efficiency:** Trade-off between optimized systems and robust ones

---

## Sample Opening Paragraph

*"In 2016, the Indian government invalidated 86% of the country's currency overnight, forcing a billion people into a digital payment system many had never used. The chaos that followed—bank queues, business failures, and over 100 deaths—revealed what a cashless transition looks like when imposed rather than chosen. Sweden, by contrast, has achieved near-total cashlessness through gradual adoption, with robust safeguards for the elderly and unconnected. These two paths illuminate the real question: not whether to fear cashlessness, but whether to fear the particular cashless society being built."*

# QUESTION 2: Technology now allows personalised pricing. If this came to be widely used, what effects should we expect?

## Question Analysis

**Question Type:** Effects Analysis/Prediction

**Key Terms to Define:**
- "Personalised pricing" - Different prices for different customers based on individual characteristics
- "Widely used" - Across most markets, not just airlines and hotels
- "Effects" - Economic efficiency, consumer welfare, distribution, social implications

**What the Question is Really Asking:**
- What happens when price discrimination becomes universal?
- Who wins and who loses?
- Is this efficient? Is it fair?
- What are the second-order effects?

---

## The Core Debate

### Potential Positive Effects

**1. Increased Economic Efficiency**
- Prices reflect true willingness to pay
- More transactions occur (some who couldn't afford uniform price can now buy)
- Reduced deadweight loss
- Better resource allocation

**2. Business Viability**
- Companies can serve price-sensitive customers without losing high-value ones
- Enables business models that wouldn't otherwise exist
- Cross-subsidization becomes explicit
- Startups can compete by targeting underserved segments

**3. Consumer Benefits (for some)**
- Lower prices for price-sensitive consumers
- Students, elderly, low-income get discounts
- More products become accessible
- Personalized offers match preferences

### Potential Negative Effects

**1. Consumer Harm**
- Those with high willingness to pay are exploited
- Urgency, addiction, or necessity used against consumers
- Privacy invasion to determine pricing
- Erosion of trust in markets

**2. Fairness and Discrimination**
- Prices based on race, gender, location (proxy discrimination)
- Wealthy areas charged more (already happens with insurance)
- Vulnerable populations targeted
- Arbitrary and opaque pricing

**3. Market Dysfunction**
- Price comparison becomes impossible
- Consumer search costs increase
- Trust in markets erodes
- Potential for collusion (algorithmic pricing)

**4. Social Fragmentation**
- No shared sense of "fair price"
- Resentment when people discover price differences
- Erosion of social cohesion
- Two-tier society: those who can game the system and those who can't

---

## Winning Argument Framework

### Thesis Option 1 (Efficiency vs. Fairness)
*"Personalised pricing would increase economic efficiency but undermine social trust. The efficiency gains—more transactions, better resource allocation—are real but modest. The costs—privacy invasion, discrimination, and the erosion of market transparency—are profound. We should expect a more efficient but less fair economy."*

### Thesis Option 2 (Distributional)
*"The effects of personalised pricing depend entirely on market power. In competitive markets, it could benefit consumers through lower average prices. In concentrated markets—which describe most of the modern economy—it would transfer surplus from consumers to firms. We should expect the latter."*

### Thesis Option 3 (Behavioral)
*"Personalised pricing exploits the gap between economic rationality and human psychology. Algorithms will learn our weaknesses—urgency, addiction, social pressure—and price accordingly. We should expect not just different prices, but prices designed to extract maximum value from our cognitive limitations."*

---

## Recommended Essay Structure

```
I. Introduction (10%)
   - Hook: You already pay personalized prices (airlines, Uber surge)
   - Define personalised pricing and its technological enablers
   - Thesis: Effects depend on market structure and regulation

II. The Efficiency Argument (20%)
   - Price discrimination theory (first, second, third degree)
   - Reduced deadweight loss
   - More transactions, better allocation
   - Acknowledge this is textbook economics

III. The Fairness Problem (25%)
   - Privacy invasion required for personalization
   - Proxy discrimination (race, gender, location)
   - Exploitation of vulnerability (addiction, urgency)
   - Use specific examples (insurance, credit)

IV. Market Structure Matters (25%)
   - Competitive markets: benefits may flow to consumers
   - Concentrated markets: benefits flow to firms
   - Most markets are concentrated (tech, retail, finance)
   - Algorithmic collusion risk

V. Second-Order Effects (10%)
   - Erosion of price transparency
   - Social fragmentation
   - Gaming and arbitrage
   - Regulatory response

VI. Conclusion (10%)
   - Net effect likely negative for consumers
   - Regulation needed: transparency, anti-discrimination
   - The question is not if but how we govern it
```

## Original Angles to Explore

1. **The End of the "Fair Price":** Argue that personalized pricing destroys the social concept of fair exchange
2. **Surveillance Capitalism:** Connect to Shoshana Zuboff's framework—pricing as behavioral modification
3. **The Arbitrage Economy:** Predict rise of services that help consumers game personalized pricing
4. **Algorithmic Solidarity:** Propose collective action through data sharing to resist price discrimination
5. **The Privacy Paradox:** People hate personalized pricing but love personalized recommendations

---

## Sample Opening Paragraph

*"When you search for a flight, the price you see is not the price. It is your price—calculated from your browsing history, your location, your device, and dozens of other signals that reveal your willingness to pay. This is personalised pricing, and it is already here. The question is what happens when it spreads from airlines and hotels to groceries, healthcare, and housing. Economic theory suggests efficiency gains; behavioral economics suggests exploitation. The answer depends on whether we live in the textbook economy or the real one."*

# QUESTION 3: Did Jeff Bezos get rich at the expense of his customers, his employees, neither or both?

## Question Analysis

**Question Type:** Distributional Analysis

**Key Terms to Define:**
- "At the expense of" - Zero-sum (his gain = their loss) vs. positive-sum (all can gain)
- "Customers" - Amazon shoppers, AWS clients, third-party sellers
- "Employees" - Warehouse workers, corporate staff, contractors, gig workers
- "Get rich" - $200+ billion net worth, mostly from Amazon stock

**What the Question is Really Asking:**
- Is wealth creation zero-sum or positive-sum?
- Did Amazon create value or extract it?
- How should we evaluate billionaire wealth?
- What is the relationship between entrepreneurial success and worker/consumer welfare?

---

## The Core Debate

### Argument: Neither (Positive-Sum)

**For Customers:**
- Amazon lowered prices across retail (the "Amazon effect")
- Convenience and selection unprecedented
- Consumer surplus estimated at $100+ billion annually
- AWS enabled countless startups and innovations
- Prime membership valued far above its cost by users

**For Employees:**
- Created 1.5+ million jobs globally
- $15 minimum wage (before many competitors)
- Career opportunities in growing company
- Stock compensation for many employees
- Jobs that wouldn't exist otherwise

**Conclusion:** Bezos got rich by creating value for customers and employees. His wealth is a fraction of the total value created.

### Argument: Both (Extraction)

**At Customers' Expense:**
- Predatory pricing drove competitors out of business
- Now raising prices with less competition
- Addictive design and dark patterns
- Third-party sellers exploited (30%+ fees)
- Data harvested and monetized

**At Employees' Expense:**
- Warehouse conditions: injuries, surveillance, dehumanization
- Union busting and anti-worker practices
- Turnover rate of 150% annually
- Wealth created by workers, captured by shareholders
- Bezos's wealth = unpaid wages of workers

**Conclusion:** Bezos extracted value from both groups through market power and labor exploitation.

### Argument: Customers Benefited, Employees Suffered

**Customers gained:**
- Lower prices, convenience, selection
- Consumer surplus is real and large

**Employees lost:**
- Warehouse workers bear the cost of low prices
- The "customer obsession" is funded by worker exploitation
- Cross-subsidy from labor to consumers

**Conclusion:** Bezos got rich by transferring value from employees to customers, keeping a share for himself.

### Argument: Employees Benefited, Customers Exploited (Less Common)

- Jobs created in areas with few alternatives
- Customers locked into ecosystem, now exploited
- Switching costs and addiction

---

## Winning Argument Framework

### Thesis Option 1 (Nuanced Both)
*"Bezos got rich at the expense of both, but not equally. Customers received genuine value—lower prices, convenience—while bearing hidden costs in reduced competition and data extraction. Employees bore the direct costs—grueling conditions, union suppression—that made those low prices possible. The question is not whether value was created, but how it was distributed."*

### Thesis Option 2 (Positive-Sum with Caveats)
*"Bezos got rich by creating enormous value for customers and, to a lesser extent, employees. But 'at the expense of' is the wrong frame. The real question is whether he captured a fair share of the value he helped create—and whether the institutions that allowed such concentration of wealth serve society's interests."*

### Thesis Option 3 (Structural Critique)
*"The question assumes Bezos's wealth reflects his contribution. But billionaire wealth is a policy choice, not a natural outcome. Bezos got rich because tax policy, labor law, and antitrust enforcement allowed it—not because customers and employees freely chose to make him rich. The expense was borne by the democratic institutions that failed to constrain him."*

---

## Recommended Essay Structure

```
I. Introduction (10%)
   - Hook: Bezos's wealth = GDP of many countries
   - Define "at the expense of" - zero-sum vs. positive-sum
   - Thesis: Both benefited and suffered, but unequally

II. The Case for Customers (20%)
   - Consumer surplus from lower prices
   - Convenience and selection
   - AWS enabled innovation ecosystem
   - Quantify the benefits

III. The Case Against Customers (15%)
   - Predatory pricing and competition destruction
   - Data extraction and privacy costs
   - Third-party seller exploitation
   - Lock-in and switching costs

IV. The Case for Employees (15%)
   - Jobs created (1.5 million+)
   - $15 minimum wage leadership
   - Career opportunities
   - Stock compensation

V. The Case Against Employees (20%)
   - Warehouse conditions and injury rates
   - Union busting
   - Surveillance and dehumanization
   - Turnover and burnout

VI. The Distribution Question (10%)
   - Value was created, but who captured it?
   - Bezos's share vs. workers' share vs. customers' share
   - Is this distribution fair or efficient?

VII. Conclusion (10%)
   - Both/and, not either/or
   - The real question is institutional design
   - Billionaire wealth as policy choice
```

## Original Angles to Explore

1. **The Counterfactual:** What would have happened without Amazon? Would customers and workers be better off?
2. **Bezos vs. Amazon:** Distinguish the person from the company—did Bezos specifically cause harm, or did the system?
3. **Time Dimension:** Customers benefited early (low prices), now suffer (market power). Employees always suffered.
4. **The Invisible Third Party:** Competitors and their employees who lost—the unseen expense
5. **Wealth as Scorecard:** Bezos's wealth measures stock price, not social contribution

---

## Sample Opening Paragraph

*"Jeff Bezos is worth more than the GDP of Greece. This wealth came from Amazon, a company that delivers packages to 300 million customers, employs 1.5 million workers, and has transformed how the world shops. The question of whether Bezos got rich 'at the expense of' these groups depends on what we mean by expense. If expense means zero-sum—his gain, their loss—the answer is complex. If expense means they bore costs that enabled his wealth—the answer is clearly yes. The interesting question is not whether value was created, but how it was divided."*
