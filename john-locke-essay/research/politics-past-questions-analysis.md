## What Judges Look For

Based on past winning essays and judge feedback:

### 1. Philosophical Sophistication (30%)
- Engage with major thinkers (Locke, Rawls, Mill, etc.)
- Understand the philosophical stakes
- Avoid superficial treatment

### 2. Argumentative Clarity (25%)
- Clear thesis stated early
- Logical structure
- Each paragraph advances the argument

### 3. Engagement with Counterarguments (20%)
- Address the strongest objections
- Don't strawman opposing views
- Show why your position survives criticism

### 4. Use of Evidence (15%)
- Historical examples
- Contemporary cases
- Data where relevant

### 5. Originality (10%)
- Fresh perspective or angle
- Not just repeating standard arguments
- Personal voice

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Definitional Evasion
**Bad:** "It depends on how you define self-determination."
**Good:** "Self-determination can mean internal autonomy or external secession. I will argue that the right to external secession is not absolute."

### 2. False Balance
**Bad:** "There are good arguments on both sides, so it's complicated."
**Good:** "While territorial integrity has legitimate claims, self-determination should prevail when peoples face systematic oppression."

### 3. Ignoring Counterarguments
**Bad:** Only presenting arguments for your position.
**Good:** "Critics argue that unlimited self-determination leads to fragmentation. However, this concern can be addressed through procedural requirements."

### 4. Excessive Abstraction
**Bad:** Discussing self-determination without any concrete examples.
**Good:** "Kosovo's 2008 declaration illustrates the remedial theory of secession."

### 5. Moralizing Without Argument
**Bad:** "Self-determination is obviously a fundamental human right."
**Good:** "Self-determination is fundamental because democratic legitimacy requires consent of the governed."

## Winning Essay Characteristics

### Structure That Works

```
1. Hook (1-2 sentences)
   - Surprising fact, paradox, or contemporary example
   
2. Context (2-3 sentences)
   - Why this question matters
   - What's at stake
   
3. Thesis (1-2 sentences)
   - Clear, specific, arguable position
   - Preview of argument structure
   
4. Body Paragraphs (4-6 paragraphs)
   - One main idea per paragraph
   - Evidence + analysis
   - Transitions between paragraphs
   
5. Counterargument Section (1-2 paragraphs)
   - Strongest objection to your view
   - Your response
   
6. Conclusion (1 paragraph)
   - Restate thesis in light of argument
   - Broader implications
   - NOT just a summary
```

### Thesis Formula for Politics Questions

**For "Is X absolute?" questions:**
> "X is a fundamental [right/principle/value], but not an absolute one. It must be balanced against [competing consideration 1], [competing consideration 2], and [competing consideration 3]. The right is strongest when [condition], and weakest when [condition]."

**For "Is X compatible with Y?" questions:**
> "X and Y are compatible only if [condition]. [Type 1 of X] can coexist with Y, but [Type 2 of X] cannot. The key distinction is [criterion]."

**For "Should we X?" questions:**
> "We should X, but only under [conditions]. The case for X rests on [reason 1] and [reason 2], but these must be weighed against [concern]. The balance favors X when [circumstance]."

## 2026 Questions: Strategic Analysis

### Q1: Is the right to self-determination absolute?

**Pattern Match:** Similar to 2021 Q2 ("Is secession ever legitimate?") and 2022 Q3 ("Can liberalism survive without nationalism?")

**What Judges Want:**
- Understanding of international law framework
- Engagement with philosophical theories (Buchanan, Moore)
- Concrete case studies (Kosovo, Catalonia, Crimea)
- Nuanced position (not "yes" or "no" but "it depends on...")

**Winning Angle:**
Argue that self-determination is fundamental but not absolute, and develop clear criteria for when it should prevail.

---

### Q2: Should states have the right to control their borders?

**Pattern Match:** Similar to 2021 Q1 ("Should we have open borders?")

**What Judges Want:**
- Engagement with both sovereignty and human rights arguments
- Understanding of cosmopolitan vs. communitarian debate
- Distinction between different types of border control
- Acknowledgment of refugee obligations

**Winning Angle:**
Argue for a qualified right that must be exercised consistently with international obligations and global justice.

---

### Q3: Is nationalism compatible with liberalism?

**Pattern Match:** Similar to 2022 Q3 ("Can liberalism survive without nationalism?") and 2023 Q3 ("Is patriotism a virtue?")

**What Judges Want:**
- Distinction between civic and ethnic nationalism
- Engagement with liberal nationalist thinkers (Tamir, Miller)
- Historical examples (both positive and negative)
- Understanding of the tension between universal and particular

**Winning Angle:**
Argue for conditional compatibility—civic nationalism constrained by liberal principles can coexist with liberalism, but ethnic nationalism cannot.

## Sample Opening Paragraphs

### For Q1 (Self-Determination)
*"In 2017, 92% of Catalans who voted chose independence from Spain. The Spanish government declared the referendum illegal and imprisoned its organizers. In 2008, Kosovo declared independence from Serbia after years of oppression; today it is recognized by over 100 states. Both cases invoked the right to self-determination; the outcomes could not be more different. This disparity reveals an uncomfortable truth: self-determination is not a right that can be exercised at will, but a principle whose application depends on context, process, and power. The question is not whether self-determination is a right—it clearly is—but whether it is absolute."*

### For Q2 (Border Control)
*"Every year, thousands of people drown in the Mediterranean attempting to reach Europe. They flee war, persecution, and poverty, seeking safety and opportunity. European states respond with walls, patrols, and deportations, invoking their sovereign right to control their borders. This collision between human desperation and state sovereignty raises a fundamental question: do states have a moral right to exclude, or is border control merely the exercise of power dressed up as principle?"*

### For Q3 (Nationalism and Liberalism)
*"In 1989, liberal democracy seemed triumphant. The Berlin Wall fell, the Soviet Union collapsed, and Francis Fukuyama declared the end of history. Three decades later, nationalism is resurgent across the liberal world—from Brexit to Trump to Orbán. This revival forces a question that liberals had hoped to avoid: is nationalism the enemy of liberalism, or its necessary foundation?"*
