## TOPIC 1: AI Governance and Regulation

### Why This Topic is Likely for 2026

- **Pattern:** Follows 2025's "Should AI-generated content be taxed?" and 2021's automation question
- **Current Events:** EU AI Act implementation, US executive orders on AI, global AI safety summits
- **Debate Intensity:** High-profile concerns from AI researchers, tech leaders, and policymakers

### Possible Question Formulations

1. "Should AI development be paused?"
2. "Is AI safety a market failure?"
3. "Should governments regulate AI like they regulate pharmaceuticals?"
4. "Is open-source AI dangerous?"

---

### The Core Debate

**Pro-Regulation (AI Safety Advocates):**
- AI poses existential risks that markets cannot price
- Rapid development outpaces safety research
- Concentration of AI power in few companies is dangerous
- Precautionary principle should apply

**Anti-Regulation (Innovation Advocates):**
- Regulation stifles innovation and competitiveness
- Risks are speculative and overstated
- Government lacks technical expertise to regulate effectively
- Authoritarian regimes will develop AI regardless

---

### Winning Argument Framework

**Thesis:** *The question is not whether to regulate AI, but how to design regulation that addresses genuine risks without stifling beneficial innovation. The pharmaceutical model—requiring safety testing before deployment—offers a template, but must be adapted for AI's unique characteristics.*

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** Frame AI governance as a collective action problem
2. **The Case for Regulation:** Market failures in AI safety
3. **The Case Against:** Innovation costs and regulatory capture
4. **The Middle Path:** Risk-based, adaptive regulation
5. **International Dimension:** Race to the bottom vs. race to the top
6. **Conclusion:** Governance as competitive advantage

---

### Key Arguments & Evidence

#### Argument 1: AI Safety is a Market Failure

**Claim:** Private incentives underinvest in AI safety because risks are externalized.

**Evidence:**
- OpenAI's safety team resignations (2024) - internal pressure to ship fast
- Anthropic research: AI companies spend <5% of R&D on safety
- Classic externality: Benefits accrue to developers, risks to society

**Counterargument:** Companies have reputational incentives to be safe.
**Response:** Reputational damage occurs after harm; prevention requires ex-ante regulation.

#### Argument 2: Regulation Stifles Innovation

**Claim:** Heavy-handed regulation will push AI development to less regulated jurisdictions.

**Evidence:**
- EU GDPR led some companies to exit European market
- China's AI development continues despite Western concerns
- Historical: Biotech regulation didn't prevent US leadership

**Counterargument:** Smart regulation can be a competitive advantage.
**Response:** EU's AI Act may become global standard (Brussels Effect).

#### Argument 3: The Pharmaceutical Model

**Claim:** AI should be regulated like drugs—tested for safety before deployment.

**Evidence:**
- FDA approval process: 10+ years, $2.6B average cost
- Resulted in safer drugs but also "drug lag" criticism
- AI differs: Continuous updates, not discrete products

**Counterargument:** AI is too different from drugs for this model.
**Response:** Adapt the principle (safety testing) not the exact process.

---

### Key Data Points

| Statistic | Source | Use In Essay |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| AI companies spend <5% on safety | Anthropic research | Market failure |
| EU AI Act: €35M fines for violations | European Commission | Regulatory teeth |
| 50% of AI researchers believe >10% chance of human extinction | AI Impacts Survey | Risk perception |
| China AI investment: $15B annually | CSET Georgetown | Competitive pressure |
| OpenAI valued at $80B+ | Press reports | Concentration of power |

---

### Original Angles to Explore

1. **AI Governance as Public Good:** Frame safety research as non-excludable, non-rivalrous
2. **Liability vs. Regulation:** Argue for strict liability instead of ex-ante rules
3. **Democratic AI:** Propose citizen assemblies to set AI values
4. **Compute Governance:** Regulate the hardware, not the software

---

### Sample Opening Paragraph

*"In 1975, molecular biologists gathered at Asilomar to voluntarily pause recombinant DNA research until safety protocols could be established. Fifty years later, we face a similar inflection point with artificial intelligence—but this time, the scientists are not pausing. The question is whether governments should force them to, and if so, how to design regulation that addresses genuine risks without foreclosing the technology's transformative potential."*

## TOPIC 2: Housing and Wealth Inequality

### Why This Topic is Likely for 2026

- **Pattern:** Wealth taxation appeared in 2024; housing is the largest component of wealth
- **Current Events:** Housing affordability crisis in most developed countries
- **Gap:** Housing has not been directly addressed since competition began

### Possible Question Formulations

1. "Is homeownership a right?"
2. "Should we tax land?"
3. "Are landlords productive?"
4. "Should governments build housing?"

---

### The Core Debate

**Pro-Intervention:**
- Housing is a basic need, not just a commodity
- Land is fixed in supply—classic case for taxation
- Speculation drives prices beyond fundamentals
- Intergenerational inequality is entrenched through housing

**Pro-Market:**
- Price signals allocate housing efficiently
- Rent control and regulation reduce supply
- Government housing projects have poor track record
- Property rights are fundamental to economic freedom

---

### Winning Argument Framework

**Thesis:** *Housing markets fail because land is fixed in supply while demand grows with population and wealth. The solution is not to suppress prices through rent control, but to capture land value gains through taxation and invest in supply—treating housing as infrastructure, not just a market.*

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** Housing as the defining economic issue of our time
2. **Why Markets Fail:** Land scarcity, speculation, NIMBYism
3. **Why Interventions Fail:** Rent control, public housing history
4. **The Land Value Tax Solution:** Henry George's insight
5. **Supply-Side Solutions:** Zoning reform, public investment
6. **Conclusion:** Housing as infrastructure

---

### Key Arguments & Evidence

#### Argument 1: Land is Different from Other Goods

**Claim:** Land supply is fixed, so price increases don't create more supply.

**Evidence:**
- UK land prices rose 544% (1995-2018) while building costs rose 54%
- Manhattan: Land is 70% of property value
- Henry George (1879): "Progress and Poverty"

**Counterargument:** Density can increase effective land supply.
**Response:** True, but zoning restrictions prevent this—a policy failure, not market success.

#### Argument 2: Rent Control Reduces Supply

**Claim:** Price ceilings discourage new construction and maintenance.

**Evidence:**
- San Francisco rent control: 15% reduction in rental supply (Diamond et al., 2019)
- Stockholm: 20-year waiting list for rent-controlled apartments
- New York: Rent-stabilized units deteriorate faster

**Counterargument:** Rent control protects existing tenants.
**Response:** At the cost of future tenants and overall supply.

#### Argument 3: Land Value Tax is Efficient

**Claim:** Taxing land value captures unearned gains without distorting behavior.

**Evidence:**
- Land cannot be moved or hidden (unlike capital)
- Singapore: 90% of land is state-owned, leased to developers
- Estonia: Land value tax funds local government
- Milton Friedman: "Least bad tax"

**Counterargument:** Difficult to separate land value from improvement value.
**Response:** Modern GIS and data analytics make this feasible.

---

### Key Data Points

| Statistic | Source | Use In Essay |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| UK house prices: 8x average income (vs. 4x in 1990) | ONS | Affordability crisis |
| 70% of US household wealth is in housing | Federal Reserve | Wealth concentration |
| San Francisco rent control reduced supply 15% | Diamond et al. (2019) | Policy failure |
| Singapore homeownership: 90% | HDB | Alternative model |
| Land value: 50% of UK national wealth | ONS | Scale of issue |

---

### Original Angles to Explore

1. **Housing as Infrastructure:** Argue for public investment like roads and utilities
2. **Intergenerational Justice:** Frame housing wealth as unfair transfer to older generations
3. **The Rentier Economy:** Connect housing to broader critique of unearned income
4. **Community Land Trusts:** Propose alternative ownership models

---

### Sample Opening Paragraph

*"In 1909, Winston Churchill declared that 'land monopoly is the mother of all monopolies.' A century later, his warning has been vindicated: in most developed countries, housing costs consume an ever-larger share of income, wealth inequality is driven primarily by property ownership, and young people face the prospect of never owning a home. The question is whether housing is a market like any other, or whether land's unique characteristics demand a different approach."*

## TOPIC 3: Industrial Policy and Economic Nationalism

### Why This Topic is Likely for 2026

- **Pattern:** Government intervention theme appears every year; trade appeared in 2019, 2023
- **Current Events:** CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act, EU Green Deal, US-China tech war
- **Debate Intensity:** Bipartisan support for industrial policy in US; global subsidy race

### Possible Question Formulations

1. "Is the return of industrial policy justified?"
2. "Should governments subsidize strategic industries?"
3. "Is economic nationalism ever efficient?"
4. "Should countries pursue self-sufficiency in critical goods?"

---

### The Core Debate

**Pro-Industrial Policy:**
- Market failures in strategic industries (semiconductors, clean energy)
- National security requires domestic production capacity
- China's success shows state-led development works
- Climate transition requires government coordination

**Anti-Industrial Policy:**
- Governments cannot pick winners
- Subsidies distort markets and invite corruption
- Protectionism invites retaliation
- Comparative advantage benefits all countries

---

### Winning Argument Framework

**Thesis:** *Industrial policy is neither inherently good nor bad—its success depends on institutional quality, policy design, and the specific market failures being addressed. The question is not whether governments should intervene, but how to design interventions that correct market failures without creating government failures.*

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** The return of industrial policy
2. **The Market Failure Case:** Externalities, coordination failures, national security
3. **The Government Failure Case:** Picking losers, rent-seeking, retaliation
4. **When Industrial Policy Works:** Conditions for success
5. **Case Studies:** Taiwan semiconductors, German Mittelstand, failed examples
6. **Conclusion:** Smart industrial policy, not no industrial policy

---

### Key Arguments & Evidence

#### Argument 1: Strategic Industries Have Positive Externalities

**Claim:** Some industries generate spillovers that markets undervalue.

**Evidence:**
- Semiconductor R&D: $1 invested generates $4-7 in spillovers (SIA)
- Clean energy: Learning curves reduce costs for all (solar down 90% since 2010)
- Defense: GPS, internet originated from military R&D

**Counterargument:** Private firms capture enough spillovers to invest adequately.
**Response:** Evidence shows underinvestment in basic research; applied research follows.

#### Argument 2: Governments Cannot Pick Winners

**Claim:** Politicians lack information and face perverse incentives.

**Evidence:**
- Solyndra bankruptcy after $535M DOE loan guarantee
- Concorde: Technological success, commercial failure
- Japan's 5th generation computer project: Abandoned

**Counterargument:** Failures are visible; successes are attributed to markets.
**Response:** True, but success rate matters. Taiwan's TSMC, Korea's Samsung show it can work.

#### Argument 3: National Security Requires Domestic Capacity

**Claim:** Dependence on adversaries for critical goods is dangerous.

**Evidence:**
- 90% of advanced semiconductors made in Taiwan (geopolitical risk)
- COVID revealed medical supply chain vulnerabilities
- Russia-Ukraine war: Energy dependence weaponized

**Counterargument:** Diversification, not self-sufficiency, is the answer.
**Response:** Diversification is a form of industrial policy—it requires government coordination.

---

### Key Data Points

| Statistic | Source | Use In Essay |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| CHIPS Act: $52B for US semiconductor production | US Congress | Scale of intervention |
| Taiwan produces 90% of advanced chips | SIA | Concentration risk |
| Solar costs fell 90% since 2010 | IRENA | Learning curve success |
| China subsidies: $300B+ for strategic industries | CSIS | Competitive pressure |
| EU Green Deal: €1 trillion over 10 years | European Commission | Global subsidy race |

---

### Original Angles to Explore

1. **Industrial Policy as Climate Policy:** Frame green subsidies as correcting carbon externality
2. **The New Mercantilism:** Argue current policies are beggar-thy-neighbor
3. **Institutional Prerequisites:** Success depends on state capacity, not just policy
4. **Friend-shoring:** Propose allied industrial policy coordination

---

### Sample Opening Paragraph

*"For forty years, the Washington Consensus held that governments should not pick winners—markets allocate resources more efficiently than bureaucrats. Yet today, the United States is spending $52 billion to build semiconductor factories, the European Union is subsidizing green hydrogen, and China continues its state-led development model. The question is whether this represents a pragmatic response to genuine market failures, or a return to the discredited policies of the past."*

## TOPIC 4: Gig Economy and Labor Rights

### Why This Topic is Likely for 2026

- **Pattern:** Labor markets appeared in 2019 (minimum wage), 2022 (UBI)
- **Current Events:** Uber/Lyft classification battles, EU Platform Work Directive
- **Relevance:** Affects millions of workers; fundamental questions about employment

### Possible Question Formulations

1. "Should gig workers be employees?"
2. "Is flexibility worth insecurity?"
3. "Should we abolish the employment relationship?"
4. "Are platforms exploitative or empowering?"

---

### The Core Debate

**Pro-Worker Classification:**
- Gig workers lack benefits, protections, bargaining power
- "Flexibility" is often employer-imposed, not worker-chosen
- Platforms externalize costs onto workers and society
- Power imbalance requires legal intervention

**Pro-Contractor Status:**
- Many workers genuinely prefer flexibility
- Classification increases costs, reduces opportunities
- One-size-fits-all regulation ignores worker heterogeneity
- Innovation in work arrangements should be allowed

---

### Winning Argument Framework

**Thesis:** *The binary choice between "employee" and "contractor" is obsolete. We need a new category—"dependent contractor"—that provides portable benefits and basic protections while preserving flexibility. The goal is not to eliminate gig work, but to ensure it is a genuine choice, not a last resort.*

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** The rise of platform work
2. **The Exploitation Argument:** Misclassification, algorithmic control
3. **The Empowerment Argument:** Flexibility, entrepreneurship
4. **The False Binary:** Why current categories don't fit
5. **Policy Solutions:** Portable benefits, sectoral bargaining, third category
6. **Conclusion:** Updating labor law for the 21st century

---

### Key Data Points

| Statistic | Source | Use In Essay |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| 16% of US workers have done gig work | Pew Research | Scale |
| Uber drivers earn $9.21/hour after expenses | MIT study (disputed) | Exploitation claim |
| 79% of gig workers value flexibility | McKinsey | Worker preferences |
| UK Supreme Court: Uber drivers are workers | Uber v Aslam (2021) | Legal trend |
| California Prop 22: $200M campaign by platforms | CA Secretary of State | Political economy |

---

### Sample Opening Paragraph

*"In 1938, the Fair Labor Standards Act established the distinction between employees—entitled to minimum wage, overtime, and protections—and independent contractors, who are not. Eighty-eight years later, this binary classification is straining under the weight of platform work, where millions of workers are formally independent but economically dependent, algorithmically managed but legally autonomous. The question is whether to force these workers into existing categories, or to create new ones."*

## TOPIC 5: Debt and Fiscal Sustainability

### Why This Topic is Likely for 2026

- **Pattern:** Monetary policy appeared in 2023, 2024, 2025; fiscal policy is related
- **Current Events:** Post-pandemic debt levels, interest rate normalization, MMT debates
- **Gap:** Government debt has not been directly addressed

### Possible Question Formulations

1. "Is government debt a problem?"
2. "Should we worry about deficits?"
3. "Can governments spend without limit?"
4. "Is austerity ever justified?"

---

### The Core Debate

**Debt Hawks:**
- High debt crowds out private investment
- Interest payments divert resources from public services
- Debt burdens future generations
- Inflation is the ultimate constraint

**Debt Doves (including MMT):**
- Governments that issue their own currency cannot default
- Debt is just money the government has spent but not taxed back
- Real resources, not money, are the constraint
- Austerity is self-defeating in recessions

---

### Winning Argument Framework

**Thesis:** *The question is not whether debt is "too high" in the abstract, but whether government borrowing is being used productively. Debt that finances investment in infrastructure, education, and climate transition can pay for itself; debt that finances consumption or tax cuts for the wealthy cannot.*

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** The debt debate after COVID
2. **The Conventional View:** Crowding out, intergenerational equity
3. **The MMT Challenge:** Monetary sovereignty changes everything
4. **The Middle Ground:** Debt sustainability depends on interest rates vs. growth
5. **What Debt Finances Matters:** Investment vs. consumption
6. **Conclusion:** Fiscal policy as investment policy

---

### Key Data Points

| Statistic | Source | Use In Essay |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| US debt: 120% of GDP (2024) | CBO | Scale |
| Japan debt: 260% of GDP, no crisis | IMF | Counterexample |
| US interest payments: $1T/year (2024) | Treasury | Opportunity cost |
| r < g for most of history | Blanchard (2019) | Sustainability condition |
| Infrastructure investment multiplier: 1.5-2.0x | IMF | Productive debt |

---

### Sample Opening Paragraph

*"In 2020, governments around the world borrowed trillions to fight a pandemic. Debt-to-GDP ratios soared to levels not seen since World War II. Yet interest rates remained low, inflation (eventually) subsided, and the predicted debt crisis never arrived. This raises a heretical question: were the deficit hawks wrong all along? Or are we simply postponing a reckoning that will be all the more severe for the delay?"*

## TOPIC 6: Food Security and Agricultural Economics

### Why This Topic is Likely for 2026

- **Pattern:** Has not appeared since 2019; environmental economics is recurring
- **Current Events:** Climate impacts on agriculture, food price inflation, Ukraine war
- **Relevance:** Fundamental to human welfare; connects to climate and trade

### Possible Question Formulations

1. "Should we subsidize farmers?"
2. "Is food a public good?"
3. "Should we tax meat?"
4. "Is food sovereignty a legitimate goal?"

---

### The Core Debate

**Pro-Intervention:**
- Food security is a national security issue
- Agriculture has positive externalities (rural communities, landscape)
- Climate change requires transition support
- Small farmers cannot compete with agribusiness

**Pro-Market:**
- Agricultural subsidies distort trade and harm developing countries
- Subsidies flow to large agribusiness, not family farms
- Price signals should guide production decisions
- Food is abundant; distribution is the problem

---

### Winning Argument Framework

**Thesis:** *Agricultural subsidies as currently designed are indefensible—they flow to wealthy landowners, encourage environmental damage, and harm developing country farmers. But the solution is not to eliminate support, but to redirect it toward public goods: soil health, biodiversity, carbon sequestration, and food system resilience.*

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** The paradox of agricultural policy
2. **The Case for Subsidies:** Food security, rural communities, externalities
3. **The Case Against:** Distortion, inequality, environmental harm
4. **Reforming Subsidies:** Payment for ecosystem services
5. **Trade Implications:** Level playing field for developing countries
6. **Conclusion:** From commodity support to public goods provision

---

### Key Data Points

| Statistic | Source | Use In Essay |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| US farm subsidies: $20B/year | USDA | Scale |
| 80% of EU CAP goes to 20% of farms | European Commission | Inequality |
| Agriculture: 10% of global emissions | IPCC | Climate impact |
| Food prices rose 30% (2021-2022) | FAO | Volatility |
| 3 companies control 60% of global seed market | ETC Group | Concentration |

---

### Sample Opening Paragraph

*"Every year, wealthy countries spend over $700 billion supporting their farmers—more than the entire GDP of Switzerland. Yet food insecurity persists, rural communities decline, and agriculture remains a major driver of climate change and biodiversity loss. The question is not whether governments should support agriculture, but whether current policies serve any legitimate public purpose, or simply transfer wealth to landowners and agribusiness."*
