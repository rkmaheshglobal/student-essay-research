# QUESTION 1: Is the right to self-determination absolute?

## Question Analysis

**Question Type:** Normative/Limits Analysis

**Key Terms to Define:**
- "Self-determination" - The right of peoples to determine their own political status and governance
- "Absolute" - Without exception, limitation, or qualification
- "Right" - Moral/legal entitlement vs. political aspiration
- "Peoples" - Who qualifies? Nations, ethnic groups, regions, individuals?

**What the Question is Really Asking:**
- Are there any circumstances where self-determination should be denied?
- What happens when self-determination conflicts with other rights or principles?
- Who gets to exercise this right, and who decides?
- Is there a hierarchy of rights in international relations?

---

## The Core Debate

### Arguments FOR Self-Determination Being Absolute

**1. Foundational Democratic Principle**
- Democracy requires consent of the governed
- No legitimate government can be imposed against a people's will
- Self-determination is the precondition for all other rights
- UN Charter Article 1(2) and Article 55 enshrine this right

**2. Historical Justice**
- Colonialism was fundamentally wrong because it denied self-determination
- Decolonization recognized this as a universal right
- Denying self-determination perpetuates historical injustice
- Indigenous peoples have inherent sovereignty

**3. Peace and Stability**
- Suppressing self-determination causes conflict (Yugoslavia, Sudan, Myanmar)
- Allowing it resolves tensions (Czechoslovakia's "Velvet Divorce")
- Forced unions breed resentment and violence
- Self-determination is the path to lasting peace

**4. Moral Autonomy**
- Peoples, like individuals, have a right to self-governance
- External rule is a form of domination
- Cultural and political identity require self-determination
- No external authority can legitimately override collective will

### Arguments AGAINST Self-Determination Being Absolute

**1. Territorial Integrity**
- States have a right to maintain their borders
- Unlimited secession leads to fragmentation and chaos
- International order depends on stable boundaries
- UN Charter also protects territorial integrity (Article 2(4))

**2. Minority Rights Within**
- Seceding groups may oppress their own minorities
- Self-determination for one group may deny it to another
- "Peoples" are not homogeneous—who speaks for them?
- Recursive secession problem (minorities within minorities)

**3. Viability and Consequences**
- Some territories cannot function as independent states
- Secession may create failed states or humanitarian crises
- Economic interdependence makes pure self-determination impractical
- International community has legitimate interests

**4. Process and Legitimacy**
- How is the "will of the people" determined?
- Referendums can be manipulated (Crimea 2014)
- External actors can manufacture separatist movements
- Unilateral secession undermines rule of law

**5. Competing Rights**
- Self-determination may conflict with human rights
- What if a people "self-determine" to oppress women or minorities?
- Democratic self-determination requires liberal constraints
- Rights of individuals may trump collective self-determination

---

## Winning Argument Framework

### Thesis Option 1 (Qualified Right)
*"Self-determination is a fundamental right, but not an absolute one. It must be balanced against territorial integrity, minority rights, and the viability of the resulting political unit. The right is strongest when exercised by historically oppressed peoples through legitimate democratic processes, and weakest when used to fragment stable democracies or create unviable states."*

### Thesis Option 2 (Conditional Absolutism)
*"Self-determination is absolute in principle but conditional in application. Every people has the right to determine their political future, but the exercise of that right must meet procedural requirements—genuine democratic consent, protection of minorities, and good-faith negotiation—that distinguish legitimate self-determination from manufactured separatism."*

### Thesis Option 3 (Hierarchy of Rights)
*"Self-determination is not absolute because no right is absolute. When self-determination conflicts with other fundamental rights—particularly the rights of minorities within the seceding territory—a hierarchy must be established. Individual human rights should take precedence over collective self-determination, meaning a people cannot 'self-determine' their way into tyranny."*

### Thesis Option 4 (Remedial Right Only)
*"Self-determination as a right to secession is not absolute but remedial. It exists as a last resort for peoples suffering systematic oppression, genocide, or denial of internal self-governance. For peoples already enjoying democratic participation within a larger state, self-determination means autonomy and representation, not unilateral secession."*

## Recommended Essay Structure

```
I. Introduction (10%)
   - Hook: Kosovo, Catalonia, Scotland, Taiwan—the question is live
   - Define self-determination and "absolute"
   - Thesis: Self-determination is fundamental but not absolute

II. The Case for Self-Determination (20%)
   - Democratic legitimacy requires consent
   - Historical justice (decolonization)
   - Peace through accommodation
   - Use examples: Baltic states, East Timor, South Sudan

III. The Limits of Self-Determination (25%)
   - Territorial integrity as competing principle
   - Minority rights within seceding territories
   - Viability and consequences
   - Process legitimacy (manufactured vs. genuine movements)

IV. When Self-Determination Should Prevail (25%)
   - Remedial secession: response to oppression
   - Democratic process: genuine referendums
   - Minority protection: guarantees for those who remain
   - Negotiated outcomes: Czechoslovakia model

V. When It Should Not (10%)
   - Manufactured separatism (Crimea, Donbas)
   - Fragmentation of functioning democracies
   - Creation of unviable or oppressive states
   - Unilateral action without negotiation

VI. Conclusion (10%)
   - Self-determination is a right, not a trump card
   - Context and process matter as much as principle
   - The question is not "if" but "how" and "when"
```

## International Law Framework

**Supporting Self-Determination:**
- UN Charter Article 1(2): "respect for the principle of equal rights and self-determination of peoples"
- International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights Article 1
- UN Declaration on the Granting of Independence to Colonial Countries (1960)
- ICJ Advisory Opinion on Kosovo (2010): unilateral declaration not illegal per se

**Limiting Self-Determination:**
- UN Charter Article 2(4): territorial integrity of states
- Uti possidetis juris: colonial borders become state borders
- Friendly Relations Declaration (1970): self-determination should not dismember states
- Most states do not recognize unilateral secession

**The Tension:**
International law simultaneously affirms self-determination AND territorial integrity, creating an unresolved tension that the essay must address.

---

## Original Angles to Explore

1. **The Recursive Problem:** If Catalonia can secede from Spain, can Barcelona secede from Catalonia? Where does it end?

2. **Self-Determination vs. Self-Government:** Argue that internal autonomy (federalism, devolution) satisfies self-determination without secession

3. **The Democratic Paradox:** A majority voting to secede may override the rights of a minority who wish to remain—is this democratic?

4. **Great Power Hypocrisy:** The same states that supported Kosovo's independence oppose Crimea's—principles or interests?

5. **Indigenous Self-Determination:** Different framework than national secession—sovereignty without statehood

6. **The Taiwan Question:** Most consequential self-determination issue today—how does the framework apply?

---

## Sample Opening Paragraph

*"In 2017, 92% of Catalans who voted chose independence from Spain. The Spanish government declared the referendum illegal, imprisoned its organizers, and imposed direct rule. In 2014, 97% of Crimeans who voted chose to join Russia. The international community declared the referendum illegitimate, imposed sanctions, and refused recognition. Both cases invoked the right to self-determination; both were denied. The difference in international response reveals an uncomfortable truth: self-determination is not a right that can be exercised at will, but a principle whose application depends on context, process, and power. The question is not whether self-determination is a right—it clearly is—but whether it is absolute."*

---

## Sample Thesis Statements

**Strong Thesis 1:**
*"Self-determination is a fundamental right but not an absolute one. It must yield to three constraints: the rights of minorities within the seceding territory, the requirement of genuine democratic process, and the principle that secession is a remedy for oppression rather than a right of first resort."*

**Strong Thesis 2:**
*"The right to self-determination is absolute in the sense that every people has a legitimate claim to determine their political future. But 'absolute' does not mean 'unconditional.' The exercise of this right must be constrained by procedural legitimacy and substantive justice—a people cannot vote themselves into tyranny or vote away the rights of their minorities."*

**Strong Thesis 3:**
*"Self-determination is not absolute because it conflicts with other rights that are equally fundamental. When a people's self-determination would create a state that oppresses its own minorities, individual human rights must take precedence. The collective right to self-governance cannot override the individual right to equal treatment."*

# QUESTION 2: Should states have the right to control their borders?

## Question Analysis

**Question Type:** Rights/Sovereignty Analysis

**Key Terms to Define:**
- "States" - Sovereign political entities
- "Right" - Moral entitlement vs. legal authority
- "Control" - Regulate, restrict, or completely close?
- "Borders" - Physical boundaries, but also citizenship and membership

**What the Question is Really Asking:**
- Is border control a legitimate exercise of sovereignty?
- Do individuals have a right to migrate that overrides state control?
- What are the limits of state authority over movement?
- Is the current international order of bordered states just?

---

## The Core Debate

### Arguments FOR State Border Control

**1. Sovereignty and Self-Determination**
- States are the political expression of peoples
- Self-determination includes determining membership
- Without borders, there are no states
- Democratic communities have the right to define themselves

**2. Social Contract and Welfare**
- Citizens have obligations to each other
- Welfare states require bounded membership
- Open borders would overwhelm social services
- "Lifeboat ethics" - can't save everyone

**3. Cultural Preservation**
- Nations have distinct cultures worth preserving
- Mass immigration can transform societies
- Existing residents have a stake in continuity
- Diversity requires distinct communities

**4. Security**
- States must protect citizens from threats
- Border control prevents terrorism, crime, trafficking
- Pandemics require border restrictions
- National security is a legitimate interest

### Arguments AGAINST State Border Control

**1. Freedom of Movement**
- Movement is a basic human freedom
- Restricting it requires justification
- Birthplace is morally arbitrary
- Why should geography determine life chances?

**2. Global Justice**
- Borders entrench global inequality
- Rich countries benefit from historical injustice
- Immigration restrictions are a form of discrimination
- "Citizenism" is like racism—discrimination by birth

**3. Economic Arguments**
- Open borders would double world GDP (economic estimates)
- Labor should flow like capital
- Immigration benefits both sending and receiving countries
- Restrictions are economically inefficient

**4. Refugee Rights**
- Asylum seekers have a right to protection
- Border control often violates refugee conventions
- Climate change will create millions of displaced people
- Moral obligation to those fleeing persecution

---

## Winning Argument Framework

### Thesis Option 1 (Qualified Right)
*"States have a presumptive right to control their borders, but this right is not unlimited. It must be exercised consistently with international obligations to refugees, respect for family unity, and the demands of global justice. Border control is legitimate; border closure is not."*

### Thesis Option 2 (Conditional)
*"The right to control borders depends on what states do with that control. A state that uses border control to maintain a just society, protect refugees, and contribute to global development exercises a legitimate right. A state that uses it to hoard resources, exclude the desperate, and perpetuate global inequality does not."*

### Thesis Option 3 (Cosmopolitan)
*"States do not have a moral right to control their borders, only a legal one. The international system of bordered states is a historical contingency, not a moral necessity. In a just world, freedom of movement would be the default, with restrictions requiring justification rather than the reverse."*

# QUESTION 3: Is nationalism compatible with liberalism?

## Question Analysis

**Question Type:** Conceptual Compatibility

**Key Terms to Define:**
- "Nationalism" - Civic vs. ethnic; patriotism vs. chauvinism
- "Liberalism" - Individual rights, limited government, rule of law
- "Compatible" - Can coexist, or fundamentally in tension?

**What the Question is Really Asking:**
- Can you be a liberal and a nationalist?
- Does liberalism require cosmopolitanism?
- Is there a "liberal nationalism" that avoids the dangers of ethnic nationalism?
- What is the relationship between individual and collective identity?

---

## The Core Debate

### Arguments FOR Compatibility

**1. Liberal Nationalism (Tamir, Miller)**
- Nations provide the solidarity needed for liberal institutions
- Welfare states require national identity
- Democracy needs a "demos" with shared identity
- Civic nationalism is compatible with individual rights

**2. Historical Evidence**
- Liberal democracies are nation-states
- National liberation movements were often liberal
- Patriotism can motivate liberal reform
- American, French revolutions were nationalist and liberal

**3. Practical Necessity**
- Global governance is weak; nations are where politics happens
- Liberal values need national institutions to protect them
- Cosmopolitanism is an elite luxury
- National identity provides meaning and belonging

### Arguments AGAINST Compatibility

**1. Fundamental Tension**
- Liberalism is universal; nationalism is particular
- Individual rights vs. collective identity
- Nationalism privileges insiders over outsiders
- Liberal equality cannot coexist with national preference

**2. Historical Evidence**
- Nationalism has led to war, genocide, exclusion
- Ethnic nationalism is inherently illiberal
- Even civic nationalism can become exclusionary
- Fascism was nationalism taken to its conclusion

**3. Cosmopolitan Alternative**
- Liberalism implies global human rights
- National borders are morally arbitrary
- True liberalism requires open borders and global justice
- Nationalism is a tribal atavism

---

## Winning Argument Framework

### Thesis Option 1 (Conditional Compatibility)
*"Nationalism and liberalism are compatible only if nationalism is civic rather than ethnic, inclusive rather than exclusive, and constrained by liberal principles of individual rights and equal treatment. Liberal nationalism is possible; illiberal nationalism is not nationalism that liberals can endorse."*

### Thesis Option 2 (Necessary Tension)
*"Nationalism and liberalism exist in productive tension. Liberalism needs nationalism to provide the solidarity that makes redistribution and democracy possible. Nationalism needs liberalism to prevent it from becoming exclusionary and oppressive. Neither is complete without the other."*

### Thesis Option 3 (Incompatibility)
*"Nationalism and liberalism are fundamentally incompatible. Liberalism's commitment to universal human rights and individual equality cannot be reconciled with nationalism's privileging of co-nationals over outsiders. What is called 'liberal nationalism' is either not truly liberal or not truly nationalist."*
