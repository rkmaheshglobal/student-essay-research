💳 Q1: Should we fear a cashless society?

Type: Evaluation/Fear Assessment · Theme: Monetary policy, Privacy, Financial inclusion

🔍 What the Question is Really Asking

The word "fear" implies a rational assessment of risk serious enough to warrant action — not mere discomfort or nostalgia for cash. Distinguish: irrational fear (technophobia) vs. rational precaution vs. structural fear (systemic risks individuals cannot mitigate).

Key Terms to Define:
  • Fear — rational precaution vs. irrational technophobia
  • Cashless society — cash-light? near-cashless? fully cashless?
  • Society — developed vs. developing countries differ enormously
Spectrum of Cashlessness:
  • Cash-light: UK, Australia
  • Near-cashless: Sweden (98% digital)
  • Fully cashless: No country has achieved this
  • Programmable: China's e-CNY experiments
✍️ Sample Opening Paragraph
"In 2016, the Indian government invalidated 86% of the country's currency overnight, forcing a billion people into a digital payment system many had never used. The chaos that followed — bank queues, business failures, and over 100 deaths — revealed what a cashless transition looks like when imposed rather than chosen. Sweden, by contrast, has achieved near-total cashlessness through gradual adoption, with robust safeguards for the elderly and unconnected. These two paths illuminate the real question: not whether to fear cashlessness, but whether to fear the particular cashless society being built."
⚖️ The Core Debate

✅ Arguments AGAINST Fear

  • Efficiency: Cash handling costs €50B/year in Europe (ECB)
  • Crime reduction: Sweden robberies fell 70% as cash declined
  • Financial inclusion: M-Pesa lifted 194,000 Kenyan households out of poverty (Suri & Jack, 2016)
  • Monetary policy: Removes zero lower bound constraint on interest rates
  • Hygiene: Cash carries bacteria; COVID highlighted this

⚠️ Arguments FOR Fear

  • Privacy: Every transaction traceable; surveillance capitalism (Zuboff, 2019)
  • Exclusion: 1.4B unbanked globally; elderly, homeless excluded
  • Fragility: 302 UK payment outages in 4 years (FCA)
  • Programmable money: China's e-CNY has expiry dates; Canada froze protesters' accounts (2022)
  • Private power: Visa + Mastercard control 80%+ of transactions
🔄 Counterarguments & Responses
CounterargumentYour Response
"Cash enables crime and tax evasion"Criminals adapt to crypto/hawala. The cost of eliminating cash (privacy, exclusion) exceeds the benefit. Most cash users are not criminals.
"M-Pesa shows digital payments are more inclusive"Context-dependent. Works in Kenya because mobile penetration > banking penetration. In developed countries, the unbanked are often also digitally excluded.
"Sweden shows cashlessness can work well"Sweden is the best-case scenario — and even Sweden reversed course. Riksbank now requires cash services; government recommends holding cash for emergencies.
"Governments won't abuse surveillance powers"Historical evidence says otherwise. Canada froze legal protesters' accounts (2022). China uses payment data for social control. Surveillance infrastructure outlasts the governments that create it.
🎯 Three Winning Thesis Options
⭐ Option A — Recommended: "Fear the Design, Not the Technology"
"We should fear not cashlessness itself, but the particular cashless societies being built. The risks — surveillance, exclusion, fragility, and loss of monetary freedom — are real and serious. But they are not inherent to digital payments; they are the result of design choices. Fear should motivate better design: privacy-preserving currencies, universal access, resilient infrastructure, and democratic accountability."
Option B: "Yes, Fear It — But Conditionally"
"We should fear a cashless society as currently designed. The efficiency gains are real but modest; the risks — to privacy, inclusion, and freedom — are profound and unevenly distributed. Until digital payment systems can guarantee the anonymity, universality, and resilience of cash, eliminating it would be a serious mistake."
Option C: "The Distributional Fear"
"Whether to fear a cashless society depends on who you are. For the digitally connected and financially secure, cashlessness is convenient and efficient. For the elderly, the poor, the marginalized, and those living under authoritarian governments, it is a source of genuine fear. A society that eliminates cash without safeguarding these groups has chosen efficiency over justice."

💡 How to Use Case Studies in Your Essay
  • Don't just describe — use each case to support or challenge a specific claim in your argument
  • Compare across contexts — Sweden (rich, high-trust) vs. India (developing, diverse) shows cashlessness is not one-size-fits-all
  • Acknowledge complexity — Kenya's M-Pesa shows digital payments can increase inclusion without eliminating cash
  • Use specific data — "86% of India's currency invalidated overnight" is more persuasive than "India tried demonetization"
  • Strongest counter-case — China shows that cashlessness + authoritarian government = surveillance infrastructure

💰 Q2: Technology now allows personalised pricing. What effects should we expect?

Type: Effects Analysis — Theme: Price discrimination, welfare, market power, data economics

🔍 What the Question is Really Asking

This question asks you to predict and evaluate effects of personalised (first-degree) price discrimination enabled by big data and AI. Distinguish between efficiency effects (total welfare) and distributional effects (who gains, who loses).

Key Terms:
  • Personalised pricing — first-degree price discrimination
  • Consumer surplus — area above price, below demand curve
  • Willingness to pay (WTP) — maximum a consumer would pay
  • Data asymmetry — firms know more about you than you know about them
Real-World Examples:
  • Amazon: prices change 2.5 million times/day
  • Uber surge pricing & route-based fares
  • Airlines: same seat, 200+ different prices
  • Insurance: telematics-based premiums
✍️ Sample Opening Paragraph
"When Amazon changes its prices 2.5 million times per day, it is not guessing. It is calculating the precise price that maximises revenue for each customer segment. This is personalised pricing in its modern form: not the haggling of a bazaar, but the algorithmic extraction of consumer surplus at industrial scale. The economic effects are profound and paradoxical — potentially increasing total welfare while simultaneously concentrating gains in the hands of firms."
⚖️ The Core Debate

✅ Positive Effects

  • Efficiency: Eliminates deadweight loss; more units sold at prices matching WTP
  • Access: Low-WTP consumers can access goods previously priced out of reach
  • Innovation funding: Higher profits fund R&D (pharmaceutical argument)
  • Competitive pressure: Firms compete on individual value, not just price

⚠️ Negative Effects

  • Consumer surplus extraction: All surplus transferred to producer
  • Privacy erosion: Requires mass surveillance of behaviour
  • Discrimination: Proxies for race/income can entrench inequality
  • Market power: Only large data-rich firms can implement effectively
🔄 Counterarguments & Responses
CounterargumentYour Response
"Personalised pricing increases total welfare"True in theory, but welfare gains accrue entirely to firms. Consumer surplus falls to zero. Distribution matters as much as efficiency.
"Airlines have always price-discriminated"Traditional price discrimination required observable proxies. Personalised pricing uses individual behavioural data — a qualitative difference in precision and scope.
"Consumers can use VPNs and incognito mode"This is an arms race consumers will lose. Firms have more data, more resources, and more sophisticated algorithms. Individual countermeasures don't scale.
"Competition will prevent exploitation"Only if consumers can compare prices. Personalised pricing makes comparison impossible — you don't know what price others are paying.
🎯 Three Winning Thesis Options
⭐ Option A — Recommended: "Efficient but Unjust"
"Personalised pricing will increase allocative efficiency but will systematically transfer consumer surplus to firms, entrench market power among data-rich incumbents, and create new forms of price discrimination that proxy for protected characteristics. The net effect depends entirely on regulatory response."
Option B: "The Data Monopoly Problem"
"The primary effect will not be efficiency gains but market concentration. Only firms with vast behavioural datasets can implement true personalised pricing, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of data, revenue, and market power."
Option C: "Context-Dependent Effects"
"Effects depend critically on market structure. In competitive markets with low switching costs, personalised pricing may benefit consumers. In markets with network effects, it will extract surplus and entrench incumbents."
📐 Theoretical Frameworks
FrameworkApplicationSource

💡 Original Angles to Explore
  • The Fairness Paradox: Personalised pricing can be both more efficient AND less fair. Efficiency ≠ justice.
  • The Privacy-Price Trade-off: Consumers "pay" for personalised pricing with their data. Is this a fair exchange?
  • Algorithmic Discrimination: If algorithms use proxies that correlate with race/income, is this illegal discrimination?
  • The Transparency Problem: You can't comparison shop if you don't know what others pay. Markets require price transparency.
  • Regulatory Arbitrage: EU has GDPR; US doesn't. Will firms relocate data processing to avoid regulation?

📦 Q3: Did Jeff Bezos get rich at the expense of his customers, his employees, neither or both?

Type: Causal Attribution — Theme: Labour economics, consumer surplus, market power, wealth inequality

🔍 What the Question is Really Asking

This question requires you to analyse the source of Bezos's wealth — was it created through value creation (expanding the pie) or extraction (taking a larger share)? The phrase "at the expense of" implies a zero-sum relationship. The best essays will challenge this framing while still engaging with genuine harms.

Key Economic Concepts:
  • Consumer surplus — value customers get above price paid
  • Monopsony power — Amazon as dominant buyer of labour
  • Rent extraction — capturing value without creating it
  • Network effects — value increases with scale
  • Predatory pricing — below-cost pricing to eliminate rivals
Amazon's Scale (2023):
  • $514B revenue; $1.3T market cap
  • 1.5 million employees worldwide
  • 38% of US e-commerce market
  • 33% of global cloud computing (AWS)
  • Bezos net worth: ~$170B (2024)
✍️ Sample Opening Paragraph
"Jeff Bezos is worth approximately $170 billion. Amazon's warehouse workers earn a median wage of $18/hour. These two facts sit in uncomfortable proximity, but proximity is not causation. The question of whether Bezos got rich at the expense of his customers or employees requires us to distinguish between wealth created and wealth extracted — between the entrepreneur who expands the economic pie and the monopolist who simply takes a larger slice."
⚖️ The Core Debate

✅ Arguments: NOT at their expense

  • Consumer surplus: Amazon created $1.4T in consumer surplus (Brynjolfsson et al., 2019)
  • Lower prices: Amazon reduced retail prices by 1-3% across the economy
  • Convenience: Prime delivery, vast selection — genuine value creation
  • AWS: Enabled thousands of startups; democratised cloud computing
  • Wages: Amazon raised minimum wage to $15/hr in 2018

⚠️ Arguments: AT their expense

  • Labour conditions: Injury rates 2x industry average (Strategic Organising Centre, 2022)
  • Monopsony: Amazon suppresses wages in regions where it dominates employment
  • Predatory pricing: Sold books below cost to destroy Borders, Barnes & Noble
  • Tax avoidance: $0 federal tax in 2018 on $11B profit
  • Surveillance: Algorithmic management, bathroom break monitoring
🔄 Counterarguments & Responses
CounterargumentYour Response
"Amazon created enormous consumer surplus"True, but consumer surplus and worker exploitation are not mutually exclusive. Amazon can simultaneously benefit consumers and harm workers.
"Amazon pays $15/hr minimum wage"Wage levels must be assessed relative to productivity and local cost of living. Amazon's productivity gains have not been proportionally shared with workers.
"Workers chose to work at Amazon voluntarily"Voluntary choice in a monopsonistic labour market is constrained choice. When Amazon is the dominant employer in a region, workers have limited alternatives.
"Bezos took risks and deserves the reward"Risk-taking justifies some return, but does it justify $170B? The marginal contribution of Bezos vs. his workers is impossible to measure precisely.
🎯 Three Winning Thesis Options
⭐ Option A — Recommended: "Both, But Asymmetrically"
"Bezos got rich both at the expense of employees and through genuine value creation for customers. Amazon created substantial consumer surplus through lower prices and convenience. However, this was partly funded by suppressing labour costs through monopsony power, algorithmic management, and predatory strategies that eliminated competitors who might have offered better employment conditions."
Option B: "Customers No, Employees Yes"
"Bezos did not get rich at the expense of his customers — Amazon genuinely created consumer value through lower prices, convenience, and innovation. But he did get rich partly at the expense of his employees, through monopsony wage suppression, unsafe working conditions, and algorithmic management."
Option C: "Neither — The Wealth is Fictitious"
"Bezos's $170B wealth is the capitalised value of Amazon's expected future monopoly rents, not accumulated surplus extracted from customers or workers. The real concern is what Amazon's dominance means for future competition and innovation."

💡 Original Angles to Explore
  • The Counterfactual: What would the world look like without Amazon? Would consumers and workers be better or worse off?
  • The AWS Question: Most of Amazon's profits come from AWS, not retail. Does this change the analysis?
  • The Stock Price Problem: Bezos's wealth is mostly Amazon stock. He didn't "take" $170B from anyone — it's the market's valuation of future profits.
  • The Pandemic Test: Amazon's value soared during COVID while workers faced increased risk. What does this reveal?
  • The Competitor Question: Did Amazon's predatory pricing harm consumers by eliminating competitors who might have innovated differently?

🌍 Q1: Is the right to self-determination absolute?

Type: Normative/Limits · Theme: International Law, Political Philosophy, Sovereignty, Secession

🔍 What the Question is Really Asking

"Absolute" means without exception or limitation. You must test whether self-determination should ever be denied, and if so, under what conditions. Distinguish between internal self-determination (autonomy within a state) and external self-determination (secession/independence).

Key Terms to Define:
  • Self-determination — internal (autonomy) vs. external (secession)
  • Absolute — without exception or limitation
  • Peoples — who qualifies? Nations, ethnic groups, regions?
  • Remedial right — secession as last resort after oppression
The Spectrum:
  • Absolute right: Any group can secede at will
  • Primary right: Peoples with distinct identity can secede
  • Remedial right: Only after serious injustice
  • No right: Territorial integrity always prevails
✍️ Sample Opening Paragraph
"In 2017, 92% of Catalans voted for independence in a referendum their own government declared illegal. In 2014, 97% of Crimeans voted to join Russia in a referendum the world condemned as illegitimate. Both invoked the same principle: self-determination. Yet one was celebrated by liberals, the other denounced. This paradox reveals that self-determination cannot be absolute — its legitimacy depends on context, process, and consequences that the principle itself cannot determine."
⚖️ The Core Debate

✅ YES — Self-Determination IS Absolute

  • Democratic legitimacy: Government requires consent of the governed; no legitimate rule without consent
  • Historical justice: Colonialism was wrong because it denied self-determination; decolonization recognized this
  • Peace through accommodation: Suppressing self-determination causes conflict (Yugoslavia, Sudan, Myanmar)
  • Moral autonomy: Peoples, like individuals, have inherent right to self-governance

⚠️ NO — Self-Determination Has Limits

  • Territorial integrity: States have right to maintain borders; unlimited secession leads to fragmentation
  • Minority rights within: Seceding majorities may oppress their own minorities; recursive problem
  • Viability concerns: Some territories cannot function as states; may create failed states
  • Process legitimacy: Referendums can be manipulated (Crimea 2014); external actors can manufacture separatism
🔄 Counterarguments & Responses
CounterargumentYour Response
"If self-determination isn't absolute, states can always find excuses to deny it"True, which is why we need clear criteria. The remedial framework provides such criteria: self-determination as secession is strongest when responding to serious injustice.
"Territorial integrity is just a tool for powerful states to maintain control"Territorial integrity can be abused, but it also serves legitimate purposes: preventing endless fragmentation, protecting minorities, maintaining international order.
"The boundary problem (who are 'the people'?) makes self-determination incoherent"The boundary problem is real but not fatal. We can use criteria like historical identity, territorial concentration, and democratic expression to identify legitimate claims.
🎯 Three Winning Thesis Options
⭐ Option A — Recommended: "Qualified Right"
"Self-determination is a fundamental right, but not an absolute one. It must be balanced against territorial integrity, minority rights, and procedural legitimacy. The right is strongest when exercised by historically oppressed peoples through democratic processes, and weakest when manufactured by external powers or used to fragment functioning democracies."
Option B: "Conditional Absolutism"
"Self-determination is absolute in principle but conditional in application. Every people has the right to determine their political future, but the exercise of that right must meet procedural requirements — genuine democratic consent, protection of minorities, and good-faith negotiation — that distinguish legitimate self-determination from manufactured separatism."
Option C: "Remedial Right Only"
"Self-determination as a right to secession is not absolute but remedial. It exists as a last resort for peoples suffering systematic oppression, genocide, or denial of internal self-governance. For peoples already enjoying democratic participation within a larger state, self-determination means autonomy and representation, not unilateral secession."

💡 Original Angles to Explore
  • The Taiwan Test: Apply your framework to the hardest case. Taiwan meets almost every criterion for legitimate self-determination, yet is denied recognition due to geopolitical consequences.
  • The Recursive Problem: If Catalonia can secede from Spain, can Barcelona secede from Catalonia? Where does it end?
  • The Democratic Paradox: A majority voting to secede may override the rights of a minority who wish to remain — is this democratic?
  • Indigenous Self-Determination: Different framework than national secession — sovereignty without statehood may be more widely applicable.
  • Great Power Hypocrisy: Same states that supported Kosovo oppose Crimea. Principles or interests?

🚧 Q2: Should states have the right to control their borders?

Type: Rights/Sovereignty · Theme: Migration, Sovereignty, Human Rights, Global Justice

🔍 What the Question is Really Asking

You must distinguish between moral right vs. legal authority, and between "control" (regulate) vs. "close" (exclude entirely). Consider both state sovereignty and individual human rights. The question is normative: should states have this right, not do they have it.

Key Terms to Define:
  • States — sovereign political entities
  • Right — moral entitlement vs. legal authority
  • Control — regulate, manage, restrict (not necessarily close)
  • Borders — territorial boundaries; who can enter/exit
The Spectrum:
  • Open borders: Free movement as default
  • Regulated borders: Control with humanitarian exceptions
  • Closed borders: Strict exclusion as default
  • Conditional right: Depends on how control is exercised
✍️ Sample Opening Paragraph
"In 2023, over 6,000 people drowned in the Mediterranean attempting to reach Europe. They died because states exercised their right to control their borders. This fact alone does not settle the question — states may have rights that produce tragic consequences — but it demands that we take the question seriously. The right to control borders is not self-evident; it requires justification."
⚖️ The Core Debate

✅ YES — States SHOULD Have This Right

  • Sovereignty & self-determination: States are political expression of peoples; self-determination includes determining membership
  • Social contract & welfare: Citizens have obligations to each other; welfare states require bounded membership
  • Cultural preservation: Nations have distinct cultures worth preserving; existing residents have stake in continuity
  • Security: States must protect citizens from threats; border control prevents terrorism, crime, trafficking

⚠️ NO — States Should NOT Have This Right

  • Freedom of movement: Movement is a basic human freedom; restricting it requires justification
  • Global justice: Borders entrench global inequality; birthplace is morally arbitrary
  • Economic arguments: Open borders could double world GDP; labor should flow like capital
  • Refugee rights: Asylum seekers have right to protection; border control often violates refugee conventions
🔄 Counterarguments & Responses
CounterargumentYour Response
"Open borders would overwhelm welfare states"This assumes immediate, complete opening. Gradual liberalization is the realistic proposal. Also, immigrants contribute to welfare states through taxes and labor.
"States have no obligation to non-citizens"This is too strong. Refugee conventions create legal obligations. Global justice creates moral claims. Rich countries benefit from global economic system that creates migration pressures.
"Cultural preservation justifies exclusion"Cultures change constantly; exclusion based on culture is discrimination. Which culture are we preserving? The "cultural preservation" argument has historically been used to justify racial exclusion.
"Security requires border control"Security concerns are legitimate but often overstated. Most migrants are not threats. Security can be achieved through targeted measures rather than blanket exclusion.
🎯 Three Winning Thesis Options
⭐ Option A — Recommended: "Qualified Right"
"States have a presumptive right to control their borders, but this right is not unlimited. It must be exercised consistently with international obligations to refugees, respect for family unity, and the demands of global justice. Border control is legitimate; border closure is not."
Option B: "Conditional Right"
"The right to control borders depends on what states do with that control. A state that uses border control to maintain a just society, protect refugees, and contribute to global development exercises a legitimate right. A state that uses it to hoard resources, exclude the desperate, and perpetuate global inequality does not."
Option C: "Cosmopolitan View"
"States do not have a moral right to control their borders, only a legal one. The international system of bordered states is a historical contingency, not a moral necessity. In a just world, freedom of movement would be the default, with restrictions requiring justification rather than the reverse."

🏳️ Q3: Is nationalism compatible with liberalism?

Type: Conceptual Compatibility · Theme: Political Theory, Identity, Individual Rights, Collective Identity

🔍 What the Question is Really Asking

This is a conceptual compatibility question. You must define both terms precisely, distinguish between different types of nationalism, and argue whether they can coexist. The key move is distinguishing civic from ethnic nationalism.

Key Terms to Define:
  • Nationalism — civic vs. ethnic vs. cultural
  • Liberalism — individual rights, limited government, rule of law, equality
  • Compatibility — can coexist without contradiction
  • Liberal nationalism — nationalism constrained by liberal principles
Types of Nationalism:
  • Civic: Based on shared values, institutions, citizenship
  • Ethnic: Based on shared ethnicity, ancestry, blood
  • Cultural: Based on shared language, culture, traditions
  • Liberal: Nationalism constrained by liberal principles
✍️ Sample Opening Paragraph
"In 1989, liberal democracy seemed triumphant. The Berlin Wall fell, the Soviet Union collapsed, and Francis Fukuyama declared the end of history. Three decades later, nationalism is resurgent across the liberal world — Brexit, Trump, Orbán, Le Pen. This return of nationalism poses a fundamental question: is nationalism the enemy of liberalism, or can the two coexist? The answer depends on what kind of nationalism we mean."
⚖️ The Core Debate

✅ YES — They ARE Compatible

  • Liberal nationalism (Tamir, Miller): Nations provide solidarity needed for liberal institutions; welfare states require national identity
  • Democracy needs a demos: Democratic self-governance requires a bounded community with shared identity
  • Historical evidence: Liberal democracies are nation-states; national liberation movements were often liberal
  • Meaning and belonging: National identity provides meaning; cosmopolitanism is an elite luxury

⚠️ NO — They Are INCOMPATIBLE

  • Fundamental tension: Liberalism is universal; nationalism is particular. Individual rights vs. collective identity.
  • Historical evidence: Nationalism has led to war, genocide, exclusion. Even civic nationalism can become exclusionary.
  • Privileges insiders: Nationalism inherently privileges co-nationals over outsiders; this violates liberal equality.
  • Cosmopolitan alternative: True liberalism requires global human rights; national borders are morally arbitrary.
🔄 Counterarguments & Responses
CounterargumentYour Response
"All nationalism leads to exclusion"Civic nationalism based on shared values, not ethnicity, can be inclusive. Scottish nationalism welcomes immigrants who share civic values. The key is whether membership is based on choice (civic) or birth (ethnic).
"Liberalism doesn't need nationalism"Liberal institutions need solidarity; nationalism provides it. Welfare states require citizens to pay taxes for strangers. National identity creates the "we" that makes this possible.
"The distinction between civic and ethnic nationalism is unstable"True, which is why liberal constraints are essential. Civic nationalism can slide into ethnic nationalism under pressure. This is why nationalism must be constrained by liberal principles.
🎯 Three Winning Thesis Options
⭐ Option A — Recommended: "Conditional Compatibility"
"Nationalism and liberalism are compatible only if nationalism is civic rather than ethnic, inclusive rather than exclusive, and constrained by liberal principles of individual rights and equal treatment. Liberal nationalism is possible; illiberal nationalism is not nationalism that liberals can endorse."
Option B: "Necessary Tension"
"Nationalism and liberalism exist in productive tension. Liberalism needs nationalism to provide the solidarity that makes redistribution and democracy possible. Nationalism needs liberalism to prevent it from becoming exclusionary and oppressive. Neither is complete without the other."
Option C: "Incompatibility"
"Nationalism and liberalism are fundamentally incompatible. Liberalism's commitment to universal human rights and individual equality cannot be reconciled with nationalism's privileging of co-nationals over outsiders. What is called 'liberal nationalism' is either not truly liberal or not truly nationalist."

💡 Original Angles to Explore
  • The Solidarity Problem: Welfare states require redistribution. Redistribution requires solidarity ("why should I pay for them?"). National identity provides solidarity. Argue that liberalism needs nationalism to sustain itself.
  • The Stability of Civic Nationalism: Is the distinction between civic and ethnic nationalism stable? Can civic nationalism resist sliding into ethnic nationalism under pressure?
  • The Immigration Test: How a nationalism treats immigrants reveals whether it's truly civic. Civic nationalism should welcome immigrants who adopt shared values; ethnic nationalism cannot.
  • The Minority Question: How does nationalism treat internal minorities? Liberal nationalism must protect minority rights; ethnic nationalism cannot.
  • The Democratic Argument: Democracy requires a "demos" — a bounded community that governs itself. Does this require national identity, or can democracy work without it?

💡 How to Use Frameworks in Your Essay

✅ Do This

  • Name the framework explicitly: "From a Rawlsian perspective..."
  • Apply it to your specific question, not generically
  • Use multiple frameworks to show you understand trade-offs
  • Acknowledge when frameworks conflict with each other

❌ Avoid This

  • Listing frameworks without applying them
  • Using a framework as a conclusion rather than a tool
  • Ignoring frameworks that challenge your thesis
  • Treating one framework as definitively correct
