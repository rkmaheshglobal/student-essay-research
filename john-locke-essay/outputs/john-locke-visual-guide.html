💲 Q1: Should we fear a cashless society?
Monetary Economics • Privacy • Financial Inclusion

Should we fear a cashless society?

Key terms: cashless societyfearmonetary freedom
⚔ The Core Debate
🔴 YES — We Should Fear It
👁 Surveillance capitalismEvery transaction tracked; governments gain total financial visibility
🚫 Financial exclusion1.4B unbanked globally (World Bank 2021); elderly, rural, low-income excluded
⚡ System fragilityPower outages, cyberattacks leave people unable to transact
🔒 Loss of monetary freedomNegative rates, spending controls, account freezes become trivially easy
🟢 NO — Benefits Outweigh Fears
💰 Efficiency gainsEliminates cash handling costs (~0.5% GDP); faster settlement
🕵 Reduces crimeTax evasion, money laundering rely on cash anonymity
📈 Financial inclusionM-Pesa brought banking to 96% of Kenyan households
📊 TransparencyReduces corruption; audit trails improve public accountability

📝 Sample Thesis Statements
Strong YES Thesis
A cashless society should be feared not because digital payments are inherently dangerous, but because the infrastructure required creates unprecedented tools for financial surveillance and exclusion that no democratic safeguard has yet proven adequate to constrain.
Strong NO Thesis
The fears surrounding a cashless society, while not unfounded, are outweighed by the concrete benefits of financial inclusion, reduced crime, and monetary policy effectiveness — provided robust regulatory frameworks accompany the transition.
Nuanced Thesis
Whether we should fear a cashless society depends entirely on the institutional context: in liberal democracies with strong data protection, the benefits dominate; in authoritarian states, the same technology becomes an instrument of control.
📄 Essay Structure (5 paragraphs)
1
Introduction ~200 words
Define cashless society. Acknowledge the trend (Sweden, China). State your thesis clearly. Preview your argument structure.
2
The Case FOR Fear ~400 words
Privacy/surveillance (strongest argument). Financial exclusion of vulnerable groups. System fragility and geopolitical risk.
3
The Case AGAINST Fear ~400 words
Efficiency and inclusion benefits. Crime reduction. Monetary policy tools. Counter the exclusion argument with M-Pesa evidence.
4
Critical Analysis ~400 words
The institutional context matters most. Distinguish between cashless by choice vs. by mandate. Role of regulation and GDPR-style protections.
5
Conclusion ~200 words
Restate thesis with nuance. Acknowledge strongest counterargument. End with a forward-looking insight about institutional design.
📈 Q2: Personalised Pricing Effects
Behavioural Economics • Price Discrimination • Welfare Analysis

Technology now allows personalised pricing. If this came to be widely used, what effects should we expect?

Key terms: personalised pricingwelfare effectsmarket power
📊 Effects Framework
🔴 Negative Effects
👤 Consumer surplus extractionFirms capture ALL consumer surplus; buyers pay their exact willingness-to-pay
👁 Privacy erosionRequires mass data collection; normalises surveillance of purchasing behaviour
⚖ Fairness concernsPoor pay more relative to income; discrimination by race/gender possible
🏛 Market power entrenchmentData-rich incumbents gain permanent advantage; barriers to entry rise
🟢 Positive Effects
📈 Allocative efficiencyPerfect price discrimination = perfectly competitive output; no deadweight loss
💰 Access expansionLow-income consumers can access goods at lower prices (airlines, software)
🌟 Innovation incentivesHigher profits fund R&D; pharmaceutical pricing enables drug development
📋 Better matchingPrices signal true valuations; resources allocated to highest-value uses
📝 Sample Thesis Statements
Efficiency Focus
Widespread personalised pricing would improve allocative efficiency and expand access to goods for low-income consumers, but only if accompanied by strong anti-discrimination regulation to prevent the technology from entrenching existing inequalities.
Fairness Focus
The widespread adoption of personalised pricing would represent a fundamental shift in market power from consumers to firms, extracting consumer surplus at scale while creating new forms of discrimination that existing competition law is ill-equipped to address.
📄 Essay Structure
1
Introduction
Define personalised pricing (3rd degree price discrimination at scale). Give examples: Uber surge, Amazon dynamic pricing, airline tickets.
2
Efficiency Effects
Welfare analysis: deadweight loss elimination vs. consumer surplus transfer. Use supply/demand diagram logic. Distinguish 1st, 2nd, 3rd degree discrimination.
3
Distributional Effects
Who wins and who loses? Firms gain, consumers lose surplus. But low-WTP consumers may gain access. Equity implications.
4
Market Structure Effects
Data as competitive moat. Network effects. Regulatory response needed. GDPR, algorithmic transparency.
5
Conclusion
Net assessment: efficiency gains real but distributional concerns dominate without regulation. Policy recommendations.
💼 Q3: Did Bezos Get Rich at Others' Expense?
Labour Economics • Wealth Creation • Consumer Surplus • Inequality

Did Jeff Bezos get rich at the expense of his customers, his employees, neither or both?

Key terms: at the expense ofcustomersemployees
💡
Key analytical move: "At the expense of" means zero-sum — one party gains ONLY because another loses. You must test whether each relationship is zero-sum or positive-sum.
👤 The Four Possible Answers
🔴
Both
Customers AND employees exploited
🔵
Customers Only
Monopoly pricing, but workers fine
🟠
Employees Only
Customers benefit, workers exploited
🟢
Neither
Positive-sum wealth creation
📊 The Evidence
🟢 NOT at Customers' Expense
📈 Consumer surplus massiveAmazon Prime saves avg. household $1,000+/yr; prices consistently below rivals
💰 Competitive pricingAmazon built market share through LOW prices, not monopoly extraction
🌟 Innovation valueAWS, Prime Video, Alexa — new products customers value highly
🔴 YES at Employees' Expense
🚫 Warehouse conditionsInjury rates 2x industry average (Strategic Organising Centre 2021)
👤 Monopsony powerAmazon = 45% of US e-commerce; dominant employer in many regions
🔒 Anti-union practicesDocumented union-busting; workers lack bargaining power to capture value created
📊 Wage suppressionMedian Amazon wage below living wage in many markets until $15 minimum (2018)

📝 Sample Thesis Statements
Employees Yes, Customers No
Bezos did not get rich at the expense of his customers — Amazon created enormous consumer surplus through competitive pricing and innovation. However, the evidence strongly suggests that Amazon's warehouse workers bore disproportionate costs through suppressed wages, unsafe conditions, and systematic denial of bargaining power.
Neither (Positive-Sum)
Bezos's wealth reflects genuine value creation: Amazon transformed retail efficiency, created millions of jobs, and delivered unprecedented consumer value. The question's zero-sum framing misunderstands how entrepreneurial wealth is generated in competitive markets.
📄 Essay Structure
1
Introduction
Define "at the expense of" as zero-sum. Introduce Amazon's scale. State your answer clearly upfront.
2
Customers: Positive-Sum
Consumer surplus evidence. Competitive pricing strategy. Network effects and innovation. Verdict: NOT at customers' expense.
3
Employees: Mixed Evidence
Wage data and injury rates. Monopsony power analysis. $15 minimum wage counterargument. Verdict: Partially at employees' expense.
4
Structural Analysis
Is this about Bezos specifically or capitalism generally? Role of regulation. Could workers have captured more value?
5
Conclusion
Bezos got rich primarily through value creation (not extraction from customers), but workers did bear real costs. The answer is: employees yes, customers no.

    💡 What Separates Winners from Runners-Up
    1
    Take a clear, defensible position
    Judges reward intellectual courage. "It depends" is not a thesis. State your view and defend it rigorously.
    2
    Use economic concepts precisely
    Name the model. Say "monopsony" not "unfair wages." Say "deadweight loss" not "inefficiency." Precision signals mastery.
    3
    Engage the strongest counterargument
    Find the best argument against your thesis and address it directly. Ignoring it signals weakness.
    4
    Use specific data, not vague claims
    "Sweden has 98% digital transactions" beats "many countries are going cashless." Specificity = credibility.
    5
    Show institutional awareness
    The best essays consider how laws, regulations, and market structures shape outcomes. Pure theory without context loses marks.
    6
    Write for a smart non-specialist
    Explain concepts briefly but don't over-explain. Assume an intelligent reader who is not an economist.
    🚫 Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • ❌Descriptive not analytical: Describing what Amazon does instead of analysing whether it constitutes exploitation
  • ❌Weak thesis: "There are arguments on both sides" — this is not a thesis, it is an observation
  • ❌Missing counterarguments: Only presenting one side makes the essay look like advocacy, not analysis
  • ❌Vague evidence: "Studies show..." without citing which studies or what they found
  • ❌Over-long introduction: Judges want your argument quickly. Get to your thesis by paragraph 1.
  • ❌Ignoring the question: Answering a related but different question. Re-read the question before every paragraph.

    💡 Writing Tips from Past Winners
    A
    Start with a hook, not a definition
    Open with a striking fact or paradox. "In 2023, Sweden's central bank warned that going cashless too fast could threaten financial stability" is better than "A cashless society is one where..."
    B
    Use the Toulmin structure
    Claim → Grounds (evidence) → Warrant (why evidence supports claim) → Qualifier (limits) → Rebuttal (counterargument). This is the gold standard for analytical essays.
    C
    End paragraphs with analysis, not summary
    The last sentence of each body paragraph should advance your argument, not just summarise what you said. Ask: "So what does this mean for my thesis?"
    D
    Cite economists by name
    Naming Pigou, Varian, or Tirole signals you have read the literature. It also gives your argument authority beyond just your own reasoning.

    🌟 Winning Strategies by Question Type
    1
    Policy Evaluation: "Should we [do X]?"
    Don't give a simple yes/no. Best answers are conditional. Framework: "It depends on [conditions A, B, C]." Define the policy precisely, identify goals (efficiency/equity), evaluate against each, consider implementation, conclude with conditional recommendation.
    2
    Assumption Challenge: "Is X always/ever Y?"
    Answer is almost always "no, not always." Find the exceptions. Identify conditions under which the assumption holds and fails. Conclude with a more nuanced principle than the question implies.
    3
    Systemic/Conceptual: "Is X compatible with Y?"
    Define both terms precisely. Distinguish between different versions of X and interpretations of Y. Argue for a specific version of X that could achieve Y. Acknowledge what would need to change.
    4
    Distributional: "Is X good for group Y?"
    Disaggregate the group. "The poor" is not homogeneous. Distinguish absolute vs. relative gains. Use specific country/group data. Conclude with conditions under which X helps or harms.
    📊 The "It Depends" Framework
    For almost any economics question, the winning answer involves these 6 factors
    🕑 Time Horizon
    Short-term vs. long-term effects? What looks bad now may be good later (and vice versa).
    📈 Scale
    Does it work at small scale but not large? Many policies fail when universalised.
    🏛 Context
    Does it depend on institutions, culture, or history? What works in Sweden may not work in India.
    👤 Distribution
    Who gains and who loses? Aggregate welfare can rise while specific groups suffer.
    ⚖ Alternatives
    Compared to what? Every policy should be evaluated against the realistic alternative, not the ideal.
    📋 Implementation
    Does theory match practice? Many good ideas fail in execution due to political economy or information problems.
    🚫 Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • ❌Taking the question at face value: "Should we tax carbon?" needs more than yes/no — what kind? At what level? With what revenue use?
  • ❌Ignoring counterarguments: Dedicate 20-25% of essay to the strongest objections and your responses
  • ❌Being too abstract: "Inequality is bad" — use specific data: "UK Gini rose from 0.25 to 0.34 between 1979 and 2019"
  • ❌Moralising without analysis: Explain WHY something is wrong economically, not just that it feels unfair
  • ❌Ignoring trade-offs: Every policy has costs — acknowledge them and explain why benefits outweigh them

    🏠 Topic 2: Housing & Wealth Inequality
    Possible Questions
  • Is homeownership a right?
  • Should we tax land?
  • Are landlords productive?
  • Should governments build housing?
    • For
    Pro-Intervention Arguments
    Housing is a basic need, not just a commodity • Land is fixed in supply — classic case for taxation • Speculation drives prices beyond fundamentals • Intergenerational inequality entrenched through housing
    Against
    Pro-Market Arguments
    Price signals allocate housing efficiently • Rent control reduces supply (Diamond et al. 2019: -15% in SF) • Government housing projects have poor track record • Property rights are fundamental
    Thesis
    Winning Argument (Land Value Tax)
    Land supply is fixed while demand grows. Solution: capture land value gains through taxation (Henry George) and invest in supply. Treat housing as infrastructure, not just a market. Milton Friedman called land value tax "the least bad tax."
    Data
    Key Statistics
    UK house prices: 8x average income (vs. 4x in 1990) • 70% of US household wealth is in housing (Federal Reserve) • Singapore homeownership: 90% via state land model • Land value = 50% of UK national wealth (ONS)
    🏭 Topic 3: Industrial Policy & Economic Nationalism
    Possible Questions
  • Is the return of industrial policy justified?
  • Should governments subsidize strategic industries?
  • Is economic nationalism ever justified?
    • For
    Pro-Industrial Policy
    Market failures in strategic industries (semiconductors, clean energy) • National security requires domestic capacity • China's state-led model is winning • Climate transition requires coordinated investment
    Against
    Anti-Industrial Policy
    Governments cannot pick winners • Subsidies invite rent-seeking and corruption • Beggar-thy-neighbor: one country's subsidy is another's unfair competition • WTO rules prohibit many forms
    Thesis
    Winning Argument
    Industrial policy is justified when correcting genuine market failures (externalities, coordination problems, national security). The question is not whether to have industrial policy, but whether governments have the institutional capacity to implement it well. Success depends on state capacity, not just policy design.
    Data
    Key Statistics
    US CHIPS Act: $52B for semiconductor manufacturing • EU Green Deal: €1T investment • South Korea semiconductors: 60% global DRAM market via industrial policy • UK industrial policy failures: British Leyland
    💼 Topic 4: Gig Economy & Labour Rights
    Possible Questions
  • Should gig workers be employees?
  • Is flexibility worth insecurity?
  • Are platforms exploitative or empowering?
    • For
    Pro-Worker Classification
    Gig workers lack benefits, protections, bargaining power • "Flexibility" is often employer-imposed • Platforms externalise costs onto workers and society • Power imbalance requires legal intervention
    Against
    Pro-Contractor Status
    Many workers genuinely prefer flexibility • Classification increases costs, reduces opportunities • One-size-fits-all regulation ignores worker heterogeneity • Innovation in work arrangements should be allowed
    Thesis
    Winning Argument (Third Category)
    The binary employee/contractor choice is obsolete. We need a "dependent contractor" category with portable benefits and basic protections while preserving flexibility. Goal: ensure gig work is a genuine choice, not a last resort.
    Data
    Key Statistics
    16% of US workers have done gig work (Pew) • Uber drivers earn $9.21/hr after expenses (MIT) • 79% of gig workers value flexibility (McKinsey) • UK Supreme Court: Uber drivers are workers (2021) • California Prop 22: $200M platform campaign
    💵 Topic 5: Debt & Fiscal Sustainability
    Hawks
    Debt Hawks View
    High debt crowds out private investment • Interest payments divert resources from public services • Debt burdens future generations • Inflation is the ultimate constraint
    Doves
    Debt Doves / MMT View
    Governments issuing own currency cannot default • Debt is money spent but not taxed back • Real resources, not money, are the constraint • Austerity is self-defeating in recessions
    Thesis
    Winning Argument
    Not whether debt is "too high" in the abstract, but whether borrowing is used productively. Debt financing investment in infrastructure, education, and climate transition can pay for itself; debt financing consumption or tax cuts for the wealthy cannot. r < g for most of history (Blanchard 2019).
    Data
    Key Statistics
    US debt: 120% of GDP (2024) • Japan debt: 260% of GDP, no crisis • US interest payments: $1T/year • Infrastructure investment multiplier: 1.5-2.0x (IMF)
    🌿 Topic 6: Food Security & Agricultural Economics
    For
    Pro-Intervention
    Food security is a national security issue • Agriculture has positive externalities (rural communities, landscape) • Climate change requires transition support • Small farmers cannot compete with agribusiness
    Against
    Pro-Market
    Agricultural subsidies distort trade and harm developing countries • Subsidies flow to large agribusiness, not family farms • Price signals should guide production • Food is abundant; distribution is the problem
    Thesis
    Winning Argument
    Current subsidies are indefensible — they flow to wealthy landowners and encourage environmental damage. Solution: redirect support toward public goods: soil health, biodiversity, carbon sequestration, and food system resilience. Payment for ecosystem services, not commodity support.
    Data
    Key Statistics
    US farm subsidies: $20B/year • 80% of EU CAP goes to 20% of farms • Agriculture: 10% of global emissions (IPCC) • 3 companies control 60% of global seed market • Food prices rose 30% (2021-2022)

    📅 Essay Writing Strategy (4 Phases)
    1
    Topic Selection (Week 1-2)
    Wait for official 2026 questions (April 1). Evaluate each question against your interests and knowledge. Choose where you can offer the most original perspective.
    2
    Research Deep Dive (Week 2-4)
    Read foundational academic papers. Gather current data and statistics. Identify 2-3 case studies. Map out counterarguments before writing.
    3
    Outline & Draft (Week 4-6)
    Create detailed outline with thesis statement. Write first draft focusing on argument flow. Ensure each paragraph advances the argument — no filler.
    4
    Revision & Polish (Week 6-8)
    Get feedback from mentors/teachers. Strengthen weak arguments. Refine prose and eliminate redundancy. Final proofread before submission.
